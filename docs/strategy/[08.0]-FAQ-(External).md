## Part A — The Basics

### Q1. What is COREcare?

COREcare is software that runs the day-to-day operations of a home care agency. One system covers scheduling, the caregiver mobile app, structured clinical visit documentation, electronic visit verification (EVV) handoff, care plans, credential tracking, payroll math (including state labor compliance), QuickBooks-ready billing with first-class invoice corrections, expense reimbursement, and a family portal. The caregiver's phone is the primary way the platform gets used in the field; office staff use a web dashboard.

We built it because every existing tool in this category was designed for a desktop world where the agency owner was the main user and the caregiver was an afterthought. That model doesn't fit the way agencies actually work in 2026. The caregiver in the field is the highest-volume user of the system, and 4 out of 5 of them quit every year. The software has to respect that reality or it becomes part of the turnover problem.

### Q2. Who is COREcare for?

COREcare is built for **independent, US-based, non-medical (private-duty) home care agencies serving 5 to 200 clients on an average daily basis**. Our sweet spot is 20–80 clients. Most of our customers are owner-operated, predominantly private-pay or LTC-insurance funded (with some VA work in the mix), and have between 30 and 250 caregivers on staff at any time.

You'll get the most out of COREcare if you're:

- An owner-operator running a single-state or small multi-state agency
- Currently fighting your software (or your spreadsheets) on scheduling, documentation, or compliance
- Tired of being treated like a second-class citizen behind franchise networks
- Considering switching off Alora because you're worried about the LivTech acquisition
- Considering switching off WellSky because you're not a franchise and it shows
- Graduating from ShiftCare or a homemade scheduling setup because you've outgrown it

### Q3. Who isn't it for?

We are honest about where we are not a fit, because evaluating us and discovering this later wastes everyone's time:

- **Medicare-certified skilled home health agencies.** OASIS-E2, PDGM 30-day episode billing, and HHVBP are not in our product. If your billing model centers on Medicare home health, look at HCHB, WellSky Home Health, or MatrixCare.
- **Hospice agencies.** Different regulatory regime. We don't do IDG meetings, hospice level-of-care escalation, or bereavement program tracking.
- **Pediatric private-duty nursing operations needing G-tube/vent/trach documentation.** Look at KanTime.
- **Franchise corporate offices.** WellSky has the franchise channel locked up. We're not built to serve that buyer.
- **Pure EVV aggregator needs.** If your primary need is to be the EVV system pushing into a state Medicaid aggregator, HHAeXchange or Sandata is what you want. We integrate with EVV aggregators, but we are not one ourselves.

### Q4. How is COREcare different from what I'm using now?

The short version: most existing tools were built around the office desktop. COREcare was built around the caregiver's phone. Everything else flows from that.

The longer version, by category:

- **vs. WellSky Personal Care (formerly ClearCare):** WellSky is the segment leader and runs 8 of the 10 largest franchise networks. If you're an independent, you're a second-class user — the roadmap serves franchise IT preferences. The codebase dates to before 2010 and the mobile app is a graft. We are mobile-first, built for independents, and our pricing page lists prices instead of saying "contact sales."
- **vs. AxisCare:** AxisCare is the most respected mid-market generalist and has an excellent API. Their UX is web-first and the caregiver mobile experience is secondary. They quote prices behind a sales call. We publish ours.
- **vs. AlayaCare:** AlayaCare targets mid-enterprise. The base price is well above the SMB lane and additional modules push the deployed price up further. We bundle aggressively and don't sell modules.
- **vs. Alora:** Alora was excellent at SMB private duty (91% customer satisfaction). LivTech acquired it on April 9, 2026, and roadmap clarity is now an open question. We're stable, independent, and committed to the SMB private-duty band as our only lane.
- **vs. ShiftCare:** ShiftCare is great at the very-small end (under 10 caregivers) and very inexpensive there, but charges per staff seat — which becomes punishing as you scale a workforce that turns over 79% per year. Documentation is also thin. We don't charge per seat and our documentation depth matches mid-market tools.
- **vs. Spreadsheets + Google Calendar + QuickBooks + group texts:** if this is your stack, you've probably outgrown it around 15–25 active clients. We're built for that transition.

### Q7. Why per active client? Why not per caregiver?

Because charging per caregiver punishes you for keeping a strong bench in a workforce that turns over roughly 79% a year industry-wide. Take a 30-client agency that hires 90 caregivers a year because most of them quit. Under a per-seat model, you're paying for all 90 every month — including the ones who left four months ago. That's not a fair deal.

Per active client matches our bill to the work you're actually billing your clients for. When you grow from 25 active clients to 75 active clients, your bill grows from $349 to about $840/month. That feels like us sharing in your growth, not extracting from your turnover.

We also looked at every other unit (per visit, per care hour, percentage of revenue, per office user only, free with payment processing fees) and they all had problems — bill volatility, perverse incentives, unfair to small agencies, or a business model that doesn't make sense for software designed to be transparent. Per active client wins.

### Q8. The "your price never goes up" promise — how does that actually work?

When you sign up, your tier price is locked. Forever. We may change list pricing for new customers in the future. Yours doesn't change.

Operationally: the day you become a customer, our system stamps your account with the pricing version that was in effect at signup (e.g., "v1.0 — May 2026"). You stay on that version as long as you're a customer. If we release a new pricing version with different prices for new customers, you're not affected. If a new version includes a feature you want, you can voluntarily migrate over — but we cannot force you.

Why we make this promise: silent annual price increases (5–10% a year is common across SaaS, with cycle resets producing 15–25%+ jumps) are the slowest-acting trust poison in software. Toast does it. Gusto raised the Simple plan 23% in March 2026. ServiceTitan has opaque escalators. We refuse the pattern. The promise is published on the pricing page so you can hold us to it.

### Q9. The annual cost-savings disclosure — what is it and what will you publish?

Every January, we publish a one-page disclosure. It contains:

- What our infrastructure cost us per customer the prior year, expressed as a range
- What it costs us this year, expressed as a range
- What we did with the difference — features we shipped, price drops we issued for new customers, or margin we kept

This document is published on our website. It is not whispered to investors. It is the public scoreboard for whether we're letting the cost savings flow back to customers.

The reason this exists: vertical SaaS companies are systematically opaque about what they make per customer and what they do with the savings as their costs drop. We think transparency on this question is a brand commitment worth more than the competitive intelligence we give up. (And we publish ranges, not per-customer line items, so we're not handing competitors a margin model.)

## Part C — Getting Started, Migration & Self-Service Onboarding

### Q13. How fast can I be live?

**14 days from contract signature to your first live, clocked visit on COREcare.** That commitment is published. It applies to every tier.

For comparison, the typical home care software implementation in this category runs 30–90 days. Some enterprise tools run six months or longer. We made 14 days a non-negotiable design constraint.

### Q14. What does onboarding look like?

Day-by-day, the typical 14-day onboarding:

- **Days 1–2:** You sign up. The system creates your tenant. We send you a welcome sequence with onboarding videos and a guided setup wizard. Your sandbox copy of the tenant is provisioned at the same time so you can experiment without affecting live data later.
- **Days 2–4:** You import (or enter) your client list, your caregiver roster, and your services. The setup wizard walks you through each step. If you have data files from another system, the migration tool reads them in. If you're connecting directly to your prior system's database or API, the importer authenticates with your credentials.
- **Days 4–7:** You build a starter set of care plans (or adopt our templates), you set up your scheduling rules and recurring patterns, and you invite your office staff. Your caregivers get app-install invites with one-tap setup. Each caregiver completes their profile (W9, TB results, CPR card, ID, background check expiration, vehicle insurance if relevant) — they cannot be assigned to a shift until their required credentials are on file.
- **Days 7–10:** Your scheduler builds the next week's schedule in COREcare. Your caregivers download the app and complete a 5-minute tutorial. Your billing settings (rate cards, overtime thresholds, mileage rate, holiday calendar) are confirmed.
- **Days 10–14:** Caregivers begin clocking visits. You watch your first dashboards populate. Your dedicated onboarding contact (yes, even at Starter — by email) checks in to flag anything that looks off.

If you stick with the prescribed flow, you're live and clocking visits inside two weeks. We track this as a P0 metric and publish our median time-to-live.

### Q15. Can I migrate from my current system?

Yes. We have specific migration support for Alora, WellSky Personal Care, AxisCare, CareSmartz360, and ShiftCare. The standard migration includes:

- **Clients:** demographics, addresses, payer info, service authorization, primary contact and emergency contacts
- **Caregivers:** demographics, contact info, hire date, service categories, credentials with expiration dates and document copies where available
- **Schedules:** active recurring shift patterns + the next 60 days of scheduled shifts
- **Care plans:** structured (goals, interventions, tasks, frequencies) where the source system supports structured export; otherwise unstructured-to-structured assistance using our care-plan AI
- **Visit history:** the most recent 90 days of completed visits with notes, for continuity-of-care purposes
- **Pay rates and rate history:** current pay rate per caregiver plus, where available, the dated history so payroll reconciliation back-references stay correct
- **Open invoices and outstanding balances:** so you have one source of truth from day one

For data we can't get out of your prior system cleanly, we'll tell you exactly what's missing before you sign anything. You won't discover migration gaps after you've started.

### Q16. White-glove migration — what does it include?

At Growth tier, white-glove migration is an optional $1,500 flat fee. At Scale, it's included. White-glove means a COREcare engineer handles the data extraction, mapping, and load on your behalf — you provide credentials to your prior system and review the results. Median completion: 5–7 business days.

White-glove customers get a structured handoff document at the end: every field that was migrated, every field that wasn't and why, the validation report from the sandbox phase, and a mapped-back source-of-truth list so you can answer "where did my X come from?" for your auditor.

Self-serve migration (using the migration toolkit with your own staff) is free at every tier and is what most Starter-tier agencies use.

### Q17. The self-service migration toolkit — how does it actually work?

The migration toolkit is a guided wizard, not a 200-page PDF. The core flow:

1. **Connect.** You give the toolkit credentials (read-only API key, database export, or CSV bundle) for your prior system. We ship purpose-built connectors for Alora, WellSky Personal Care, AxisCare, CareSmartz360, and ShiftCare. For other systems we accept CSV bundles in our published schema.
2. **Inspect.** The toolkit reads what's there and produces a side-by-side preview: every record type, every field, what it maps to in COREcare, what doesn't map, and why. You see the report before any data is committed.
3. **Map.** For fields that don't auto-map (custom fields, agency-specific labels, prior-system quirks), you map them yourself in a UI — drag the source field to the target field, set a default if it's blank, set a transformation if the values don't line up (e.g., "Aide" → "HHA").
4. **Workflow mapping.** See Q18. The toolkit also asks you about your *processes* — how you track call-outs today, how you handle invoice corrections today, how you do payroll today — and configures the matching COREcare workflows for you, so you don't land in v2 with a clean dataset and a blank UI.
5. **Validate.** The toolkit loads everything into your sandbox tenant. You poke around. You run a fake schedule, generate a fake invoice, click through a fake clock-in. You confirm it looks right.
6. **Commit.** When you say go, the toolkit promotes the sandbox tenant to live. The whole process is logged. If anything was edited in the sandbox during validation, those edits are preserved.

You do not write code. You do not call us in a panic. The wizard works at the pace of your decision-making.

### Q18. Workflow mapping — how do I bring my processes into COREcare?

Migration is more than data — it's also process. A clean import of records doesn't help if your scheduler still has to relearn how to handle a 6 AM call-out, how to issue a corrected invoice, how to approve an expense, or how to onboard a new caregiver.

The workflow-mapping step of the toolkit asks targeted questions about how you do these things today, and configures the matching COREcare workflow:

- **Call-outs and shift broadcasts.** "When a caregiver calls out, who finds the replacement? Do you broadcast to a pool, or hand-pick? Do you require manual approval before the swap is final?"
- **Invoice corrections.** "When an invoice goes out wrong, do you void-and-reissue, do you issue a credit memo, or do you ship a revised invoice with the same number? Do you require manager sign-off?"
- **Expense approvals.** "Who approves caregiver expenses? Is there a per-amount threshold above which a second approver is required?"
- **Caregiver onboarding.** "What credentials are required before a caregiver can clock in? Is there a probationary period?"
- **Family access.** "Do you grant family portal access proactively to every client's primary contact, or only on request? Do you require a signed consent before granting access?"

For each question, the toolkit shows you the COREcare default, your prior-system default (where we can detect it), and lets you pick. You can change any of these later in System Settings — see Q93.

### Q19. The pre-migration sandbox — what is it and why does it matter?

Every COREcare tenant comes with a sandbox copy that mirrors the structure of your live tenant but lives in a separate, clearly-labeled environment. During migration, we load your imported data into the sandbox first. You poke at it. You schedule a fake shift. You generate a fake invoice. You make sure your scheduler doesn't show up Monday and find that all the addresses are wrong.

The sandbox stays available after go-live, too — you can use it to train new office staff, dry-run a process change, test how a rate change will flow through the next pay period, or experiment with a new care-plan template without touching live data. Sandboxes share schema and feature parity with live but are isolated at the data layer; nothing you do in sandbox affects live.

### Q20. What happens if a migration goes sideways? Can I roll it back?

Yes. Until you commit the sandbox to live, nothing is real. You can re-run the migration as many times as you want, change the field mappings, reload, re-validate, and commit only when you're satisfied.

After commit: for the first 30 days, we keep a snapshot of your pre-migration tenant state. If something fundamental turns out to be wrong, we can roll the live tenant back to that snapshot. After 30 days, we keep an archive copy for 90 more days but full restoration becomes a per-engagement support exercise (still free; just not a one-click revert).

### Q21. What if my prior system isn't on your supported list?

We accept generic CSV bundles in our published schema. The schema is documented at developer.corecare.com and includes columns and data types for clients, caregivers, schedules, care plans, credentials, visit history, rates, and open invoices. Most prior systems can produce CSVs that match (sometimes after a transform pass; the toolkit includes a mapping step for that, see Q17).

If your prior system can't produce CSVs and doesn't have an API, white-glove is your option — our engineer will work with whatever export format your vendor will provide (including printed reports as a last resort, though it's slow).

We're also actively expanding the list of supported direct-connector imports. If you have a system you'd like us to support, tell us during evaluation. We've prioritized direct connectors for the systems agencies actually leave from.

## Part D — The Caregiver Mobile App

### Q22. What can my caregivers do in the app?

Everything they need to do their job, without ever logging into a desktop:

- View today's and the upcoming week's shifts, with client info and address
- Clock in and clock out of visits
- Complete visit documentation — ADL checklists, structured clinical entries (vital signs, glucose, intake/output, medication administration), observation notes (typed or dictated, with AI structuring help)
- View the active care plan for each client they're assigned to, including the medication master list and any per-visit care plan tasks
- See which credentials they hold and which are expiring (with the upload affordance built in)
- Request shift swaps with eligible coworkers
- Message the scheduler or supervisor (PHI-safely; no need to use personal text)
- See hours-to-date, mileage-to-date, and a pay-stub-to-date view
- Submit expenses (out-of-pocket or company-card) with receipt photos
- (At your option) view earned-wage-access integration if you offer DailyPay/Tapcheck

The app is designed to make every common task possible in under three taps. Clock-in is one tap from the home screen.

### Q23. How does clock-in work?

The caregiver opens the app, taps the upcoming or current visit, and taps **Clock In**. That's it.

Behind the scenes, COREcare records the timestamp, the device, and a verification token (see Q24) and creates a visit record. The same flow happens at clock-out.

If the caregiver clocks in late, the app prompts a quick reason code (traffic / late start / client change / other) which feeds your dashboard's punctuality reporting without making the caregiver fill out a form.

If the caregiver hasn't yet accepted the shift when they try to clock in (acceptance got missed), the app surfaces a one-tap acceptance modal at clock-in time so the workflow doesn't break — and the response channel is recorded for your records.

### Q24. Do you track caregiver location? GPS?

**No — and this is intentional.** We don't track real-time GPS as a condition of employment. Caregivers are professionals; treating them like a delivery package is the wrong default and contributes to turnover.

For visit verification, we use one or more of these mechanisms (you choose at setup which mix you want):

- **Client signature** on the caregiver's phone at arrival and departure
- **Photo verification** (e.g., a photo of the home interior posted-Polaroid-style — never of the client unless the client opts in)
- **NFC tag** placed at the client's home that the caregiver taps the phone against
- **Wi-Fi fingerprint** of the client's home network (recognized but not retained centrally)
- **Manual confirmation** by the client or family member through the family portal

For agencies serving HCBS waiver work where state EVV requires a GPS-style verification, we push verified-visit records into the state aggregator (see Q82) — but the caregiver isn't being surveilled by COREcare itself.

### Q25. What if a caregiver works in a basement / no signal?

The app works offline. Caregivers can clock in, complete visit documentation (including structured clinical entries), capture verification artifacts, and submit expenses with photos without a data connection. The moment the phone reconnects to a network, the data syncs. This is built in at the architecture layer; it's not a bolt-on.

In our internal testing, an offline-completed visit (clock-in, full documentation, clock-out, photo verification) syncs in under 4 seconds when the connection returns.

### Q26. What devices does the app work on?

iPhone (iOS 15+) and Android (Android 10+, with broad support for older Samsung and Google phones common among caregivers). The app is intentionally designed to work well on older mid-range Android devices because that's what most caregivers actually use.

We also publish task-level latency benchmarks: the median clock-in completes in under 2 seconds on a 2020 Samsung A12 over LTE.

### Q27. How do caregivers do visit notes?

Three options — caregiver chooses per visit:

1. **Structured fast path:** the care plan presents a checklist (ADLs completed, vitals, tasks). Caregiver taps through. Total time: 30–90 seconds for a typical companionship visit.
2. **Unstructured + AI structuring:** caregiver types or dictates a short note about what happened. The AI organizes the content into ADL checklist entries, vitals, observation categories, and a free-text observation field. Caregiver reviews and accepts (or edits) before submitting.
3. **Voice-to-text hands-free:** for caregivers actively performing tasks, voice dictation is captured during the visit and structured at the end. (P3 feature; available in v1.x.)

In all three modes, the documentation is reviewable and editable by the caregiver before submission. AI does not submit on the caregiver's behalf.

For clinical structure beyond ADLs (vital signs, glucose readings, intake/output, medication administration events), see Q56.

### Q28. Can caregivers message the office without giving out their personal phone?

Yes. The app has a built-in messaging surface for caregiver-to-scheduler and caregiver-to-supervisor communication. Messages are PHI-safe (you cannot include client names or identifiers in messages — the system flags them at send time and offers safer alternatives). All messages are logged for audit purposes.

This eliminates the most common privacy and compliance risk in home care: caregivers using personal SMS for work coordination, with PHI flowing across uncontrolled channels.

### Q29. How do shift swaps work?

A caregiver who needs to swap a shift opens the shift in the app and taps **Request Swap**. The system shows eligible coworkers (right credentials, no scheduling conflict, geographically reasonable). The caregiver picks one or more, the system sends a request, and the receiving caregiver accepts or declines in-app. Once accepted, the swap goes to the scheduler for one-tap approval (or auto-approval, your choice — set at the agency level in System Settings).

No phone trees. No "did you get my text?" The whole flow is logged, including who initiated, who accepted, and who approved.

### Q30. How does the caregiver see what they've earned this period?

Inside the app, the caregiver has an "Earnings" view that shows hours-to-date, mileage-to-date, projected gross pay (regular + overtime + mileage + any approved expenses pending reimbursement), and the close date for the current pay period. Once a pay period closes and you've approved payroll, the historical period appears alongside.

This eliminates the most common caregiver-side payroll question ("what did I make this week?") without requiring the caregiver to call the office.

## Part E — Scheduling & Operations

### Q31. How does scheduling work?

The scheduler uses a single-screen view in the web dashboard showing:

- Caregiver availability for the time window you're scheduling
- Each caregiver's credential status (any expiring credentials are flagged; expired-required credentials make the caregiver unschedulable for matching client types — see Q34)
- Geographic distance from each caregiver's primary location to the client
- Existing schedule for each caregiver in that window
- Conflict warnings (overtime exposure, double-booking, credential gaps, client-caregiver-mismatch, meal-period violation risk)

The scheduler drag-and-drops or click-assigns shifts. The system never lets you publish a schedule that breaks at runtime — every conflict is surfaced before the schedule goes live.

For recurring patterns (Mr. Smith on Mondays/Wednesdays/Fridays, 9 AM–1 PM, every other week), you set the pattern once and the system generates the schedule into the future, with exception handling for holidays and caregiver time-off.

### Q32. What happens when a caregiver calls out at 6 AM?

The 6 AM call-out is the moment a scheduling tool either earns its keep or doesn't. Here's the COREcare flow:

1. Caregiver opens the app, taps the shift, taps **Cannot Work**, picks a reason code.
2. Scheduler gets a notification (push + dashboard alert + in-app inbox entry — see Q91).
3. Scheduler opens the shift in the dashboard. The system shows eligible caregivers — credentialed for this client, available in the window, geographically reasonable, not at risk of overtime.
4. Scheduler taps **Broadcast** and the open shift goes to those caregivers as a push notification + SMS.
5. First eligible caregiver to tap **Accept** gets the shift. Others see "filled" and stop being prompted.
6. Client and family (per their notification settings) are notified that a different caregiver is coming.

A 15-phone-call morning is now a 15-minute window. The full event is logged: who declined, who was offered, who accepted, when, on what response channel.

### Q33. How are caregiver credentials tracked?

Every caregiver has a credentials section listing licenses (state-specific), CPR certification, TB test results, background check expiration, vehicle insurance (if relevant), liability insurance, W9, ID verification, signed contract, and any agency-required training certifications. The full list of standard credential types covers both the 1099 onboarding checklist and the nurse onboarding checklist.

For each credential, COREcare stores the issue date, expiration date, and a copy of the document. Documents are uploaded by the caregiver from the phone or by office staff from the dashboard.

The system runs nightly checks on expirations. Caregivers and schedulers receive alerts at 30, 14, and 7 days before expiration, and on the day of expiration. (Notification surfaces: in-app inbox, email, optional SMS — see Part N.)

### Q34. How do credential gates work in the schedule?

A caregiver with an expired *required* credential becomes unschedulable for client types requiring that credential — the scheduler cannot assign them to those shifts at all. This is a hard schedule gate, not a warning.

If you need to override the gate in an emergency (e.g., the only available caregiver has a CPR certification that just expired and the renewal class is tomorrow), the override is logged with the override reason, the approving manager, and the expiration date of the override.

You configure which credentials are required per client type in System Settings. The defaults match common state private-duty home care requirements; you adjust to match your service mix.

### Q35. How are recurring shifts and exceptions handled?

You set the recurrence pattern once: client, caregiver(s), days of the week, time window, recurrence rule (weekly, bi-weekly, every-other-weekend, custom), end date or open-ended. The system generates the calendar.

For exceptions (a caregiver's vacation week, a client's hospital admission, a holiday closure), you mark the exception once and the system handles substitution requests, schedule blanking, or ripple effects automatically.

You don't rebuild the schedule every week.

### Q36. What does the owner / scheduler dashboard show?

The owner dashboard surfaces operational health at a glance:

- **Fill rate** today / this week / this month, with trend
- **Open shifts** that need filling, sorted by urgency
- **Overtime exposure** (which caregivers are tracking toward overtime this week)
- **Caregiver activity** (who's clocked in, who's late, who hasn't responded to a swap request)
- **Documentation completeness** by caregiver and by client
- **Credential expirations** in the next 30 days
- **Active client count vs. tier** (so you know if you're approaching overage)
- **Pending approvals** (expenses, shift swaps, payroll, health reports awaiting sign-off)
- **Care manager action queue** (no-shows, overdue care requests, vital trends — see Q63)
- **Vendor support sessions** (any active or recent COREcare staff impersonation into your tenant — see Q88)

The scheduler dashboard adds shift-planning and call-out fill workflows. You can build custom views for additional metrics if you're at Growth or Scale.

## Part F — Caregiver Pay & Labor Compliance

### Q37. How are pay rates structured?

Each caregiver has a current pay rate. The pay rate is recorded with an effective date (rates change over time, and historical accuracy matters for payroll reconciliation). On every shift, the system records both the caregiver's pay rate at that moment and the bill rate for the client (see Q70 — these are different and that's deliberate).

The pay rate can vary by service category if you assign different rates for, say, companion care vs. personal care vs. RN-supervised visits. You configure the rate-by-category structure in System Settings; the scheduler picks the right one automatically based on the client and service type.

### Q38. How do scheduled (effective-dated) rate changes work?

Most agencies need to be able to change pay rates without retroactively rewriting history — a rate change announced in March that takes effect on April 1 must apply to April 1 paychecks, not March paychecks.

In COREcare, you schedule a rate change with an effective date. The change sits as "pending" until the effective date arrives. On that date, an automated job applies it to the caregiver's record. Visits before the effective date use the old rate; visits on and after use the new rate. Payroll reconciliation works correctly across the boundary.

This is the right behavior for a few reasons that matter:

- **State minimum wage updates.** When a state raises minimum wage on January 1, you can schedule the corresponding rate floor change in advance and the system applies it on January 1 without you remembering.
- **Annual review cycles.** When you give a caregiver a raise effective on their hire-date anniversary, the change takes effect on that date, not the date you got around to entering it.
- **Audit trail.** Every rate change records who scheduled it, when, with what effective date, and who applied it (the system, automatically). Historical paychecks can be reconciled to the rate that was in effect on the visit date.

### Q39. Overpayment consent and paycheck deductions — what's the workflow?

Sometimes payroll mistakes happen — a duplicated visit, a miscalculated overtime line, a mileage entry that turned out not to qualify. Recovering those overpayments is a labor-law minefield in some states; in California and several others, employers cannot unilaterally deduct from a future paycheck without written employee consent.

COREcare handles this as a structured workflow:

1. The overpayment is flagged in the system (manually by payroll staff, or automatically by the reconciliation jobs).
2. An overpayment-consent record is generated with the amount, the reason, the period it covers, and a proposed deduction schedule (lump-sum or amortized over multiple paychecks).
3. The caregiver receives the consent in-app and on email. They can accept (e-signature, timestamped, IP-logged), reject, or propose alternative terms.
4. Only after acceptance does the deduction get applied to the next-relevant paycheck. Pre-acceptance, no deduction is queued.
5. Every step is in the audit log.

Your payroll exports — to QuickBooks, Gusto, Rippling, or CSV — already have the deduction line item built in.

### Q40. Meal periods and rest breaks — how does COREcare handle them, including waivers?

State labor law on meal and rest breaks varies widely. California, Oregon, Nevada, Kentucky, and New Jersey have hard rules (CA: 30-min unpaid meal break for shifts over 5 hours, second 30-min for shifts over 10 hours, premium pay if missed). Several other states are softer. Some live-in or 24-hour-coverage arrangements legally allow caregiver waivers.

COREcare's approach:

- **Tracking.** When a shift is configured to require a meal period, the caregiver app prompts at the appropriate time. The break window is tracked automatically. If the break is missed, the system records a missed-break event and surfaces it in payroll for premium-pay computation.
- **Waivers.** For shift types where state law allows the caregiver to waive the meal period (e.g., live-in, certain on-call configurations), you generate a waiver from the system. The caregiver e-signs in-app. The signed waiver is stored with the caregiver's compliance documents. While the waiver is active, the prompt is suppressed for matching shift types.
- **Compliance reporting.** When a state surveyor or labor-law audit asks "show me your break-tracking records for the audit window," you generate the report from the dashboard. Shifts, breaks taken, missed breaks, premium-pay applications, and active waivers are all in one place.

You configure the per-state rule set in System Settings. The defaults match each state's letter of the law; you can tighten (some agencies offer more generous breaks than required) but not loosen below the legal floor.

### Q41. Mileage pay and per-trip reimbursement — how is that calculated and paid?

If you reimburse caregivers for client-related mileage, COREcare handles it as a first-class part of payroll, not a manual line-item-add. The flow:

- The caregiver enters trip distance at clock-in/clock-out (or the office staff enters it from receipts/odometer reports).
- The system applies the configured mileage rate from System Settings (typically the IRS standard rate or your agency's published rate).
- Mileage pay aggregates per pay period as a separate line item from regular hourly pay (mileage is generally non-taxable up to the IRS rate, which the payroll export respects).
- The caregiver sees mileage-to-date in the app's Earnings view (see Q30).
- The mileage rate is a configurable system setting. When the IRS updates the rate, you change it once and the change applies on the effective date you set (see Q93 on confirmable system settings).

### Q42. How do payroll, overtime, and pay periods get computed?

Visit hours are aggregated automatically into pay-period buckets. The system computes regular hours, overtime hours (per your state's rules), holiday hours (per your holiday calendar), mileage pay, expense reimbursements that have been approved and assigned to this pay period, and any consented overpayment deductions.

Pay-period rollovers are automated. The output is a payroll-ready package you can review, approve, and export to your payroll processor (or download as CSV/PDF). The package separates: regular pay, overtime pay, holiday pay, mileage pay, expense reimbursements, deductions, taxes (where you handle them in COREcare), and net pay.

The overall payroll workflow has segregation of duties: the staff member who *creates* the period draft is not the same person who *approves* it for export. This is configurable but on by default and cannot be disabled without an explicit, audit-logged confirmation. Every state of the period is tracked: draft → submitted → approved → exported, each with a timestamp, actor, and reason.

QuickBooks integration takes the same data and posts payroll/billing entries directly to your QuickBooks account, eliminating the manual transcription step that's the #1 source of payroll errors in home care.

### Q43. Caregiver titles, employee IDs, and the title-to-permission relationship

Every caregiver has a standardized title — RN, LPN/LVN, CNA, HHA, lead caregiver, MD, NP, OT, PA, PT, RT, admin, or other — and a system-generated employee ID. Titles are not free text; they come from a controlled list. Why this matters:

- **Payroll exports** map cleanly to your payroll processor's job-classification fields.
- **Credential gates** can require specific titles for specific client types (e.g., a skilled-care visit requires an RN/LVN title and a current state license).
- **AI scheduling fit** uses titles as one of the inputs when ranking eligible caregivers.
- **Compliance reporting** can answer "show me every visit performed by an RN over this period."
- **Future role-permission tie-ins** are anchored to titles (we do not promise a v1.0 implementation of title-based permissions; the data model supports it for v1.x).

Employee IDs are auto-generated, sequential, and never reused. If a caregiver leaves and returns, they get a new caregiver record and a new ID — old visit history is preserved against the original record for payroll reconciliation.

## Part G — Expense Management

### Q44. How does expense reimbursement work for caregivers and care managers?

Caregivers and care managers regularly spend money on behalf of clients (groceries, supplies, medication co-pays, equipment, training fees, transportation costs that aren't covered by mileage pay) or on behalf of the agency (admin supplies, meals during long shifts, etc.). COREcare has a unified expense workflow rather than treating these as one-off invoice line items or off-platform reimbursements.

The flow:

1. The submitter (caregiver or care manager) opens the **Submit Expense** screen in the mobile app or web dashboard.
2. They select an expense type from a controlled list (supplies, equipment, transportation, meals, training, medical, groceries, admin, other).
3. They enter amount, date, the client (if client-related), and a brief description.
4. They attach a receipt photo or PDF (required by default; see Q46).
5. They mark the payment method: out-of-pocket (you owe me) or company card (record-only).
6. They submit. The expense enters the approval queue.

The submitter sees the status update in real time: submitted → pending → approved → processed → reimbursed, with timestamps. If the expense is rejected or adjusted, they see the reason.

### Q45. Who submits, who approves, who pays?

- **Submission** can come from the caregiver (typically out-of-pocket purchases like groceries for a client), the care manager (typically client-related travel and supplies), or office staff (typically agency-owned costs).
- **Approval** happens at the role level you configure in System Settings — you can require manager approval for any caregiver-submitted expense, or only above a threshold, or require a second approver above a higher threshold (segregation of duties).
- **Payment** is either:
  - **Out-of-pocket**: the approved expense rolls into the next payroll cycle as a reimbursement line item, paid alongside regular wages.
  - **Company card**: the expense is recorded for accounting purposes but no payment is owed (the card already paid the merchant).
- **Adjustment**: if an approved expense needs to be corrected after the fact (e.g., a receipt was duplicated, a wrong client was assigned), the adjustment is captured with a required edit reason and the original amount is preserved for audit.

### Q46. Receipts, payment methods, and the audit trail on expenses

- **Receipts**: required by default. The system enforces "no receipt, no submission" unless you explicitly disable the requirement (some agencies allow under-$5 expenses without receipts; that's configurable). Receipts are stored with HIPAA-aware filename handling — if the original filename contains anything that looks like client identifiers, the system renames it.
- **Payment methods**: out-of-pocket vs. company card. The distinction drives whether the expense triggers a reimbursement line or is bookkeeping-only. You can also set a default per submitter role (most caregivers default to out-of-pocket; most office staff default to company card).
- **Audit trail**: every state change (submitted, approved, adjusted, rejected, reimbursed) is logged with timestamp, actor, and reason where applicable. The audit log entry is queryable from the admin audit log UI (Growth/Scale) and exportable (Scale).

The expense workflow is flexible enough to handle small everyday purchases without overhead and rigorous enough to satisfy the auditor who asks "show me every reimbursement to caregiver X over Q3."

## Part H — AI Features

### Q47. What does the AI actually do for me?

Three places. AI helps with:

1. **Visit documentation** — turning a caregiver's quick note into structured documentation (ADL checklist entries, vitals capture, observation categorization)
2. **Scheduling** — flagging the best-fit caregivers for an open shift based on history, credentials, location, and prior client compatibility
3. **Care plans** — drafting initial care plans from intake notes, flagging clinical-decline patterns in observations, suggesting interventions

In all three places, AI suggests; humans decide. The caregiver approves the visit note. The scheduler picks who to send the shift to. The supervisor accepts, modifies, or rejects care plan suggestions. AI doesn't act on its own.

### Q48. AI for visit documentation — how does it work?

The caregiver types or dictates a short note about what happened during the visit. For example: *"Margaret was a little more tired today, ate about half her lunch, took her morning meds with no fuss, asked twice when her son was visiting. Walked to the porch with the walker, no shortness of breath. Toileted twice with no issues. Tidied the kitchen and started her laundry."*

The AI produces a structured version:

- **ADL checklist:** mobility (with assist) — completed; toileting — completed; meal prep / eating — partial intake noted; hygiene — not addressed this visit; medication assist — completed
- **Vitals (non-clinical):** none captured this visit
- **Observations:** mild fatigue noted; reduced appetite (50% lunch); no respiratory distress on ambulation
- **Cognitive/affective:** repeated questions about son's visit (frequency: 2)
- **Free-text note:** preserved verbatim

The caregiver reviews this on screen, edits anything that doesn't match, and submits. Total time from finishing the visit to submitted documentation: typically 60–90 seconds.

### Q49. AI for scheduling — how does it work?

When a scheduler opens an unfilled shift, the system surfaces a ranked list of eligible caregivers with reasoning. For example:

> **Maria S.** — strong fit (worked with this client 8 prior visits, lives 12 minutes away, all credentials current, no overtime risk this week)
> **James T.** — moderate fit (credentialed, 22 minutes away, no prior history with client)
> **Rachel L.** — possible fit (credentialed, 8 minutes away, but at 38/40 weekly hours — overtime risk)

The scheduler picks. The AI doesn't auto-assign. The reasoning is shown so the scheduler can sanity-check and develop intuition for the suggestions.

### Q50. AI for care plans — how does it work?

Two surfaces:

- **Care plan drafting:** at intake, the AI takes the assessment notes, client preferences, family input, and (where present) physician orders, and drafts a structured care plan with goals, interventions, and tasks. The supervisor reviews, edits, and finalizes. This converts a 90-minute care plan authoring task into a 20-minute review.
- **Trend surfacing:** the AI watches incoming visit observations across days/weeks and flags patterns — sudden ADL decline, fall events mentioned in notes, caregiver-reported client distress, repeated medication refusal. Flags appear in the supervisor dashboard with the underlying observations linked. This is not a clinical diagnosis; it's a noticed pattern that prompts a human review. The deterministic action queue (no-shows, overdue care requests, vital threshold crossings — see Q63) runs alongside, not replaced by AI.

### Q51. Is my data used to train AI models? (No.)

No. Your client and caregiver data is never used to train any AI model — ours, the AI provider's, or anyone else's. We have a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with our AI provider that explicitly excludes your data from training corpora. This is a non-negotiable contractual term, not a policy that can be changed by a roadmap update.

### Q52. Can I review and edit what AI generates?

Always. Every AI output is reviewable and editable by the human in the loop before it's submitted. The caregiver edits the visit note. The supervisor edits the care plan. The scheduler ignores the AI ranking if their judgment differs.

In every case, the system records what the AI generated, what the human submitted, and the difference. This serves two purposes: it gives you an audit trail of human decision-making, and it lets us improve the AI's suggestions over time without ever retraining on your specific data.

### Q53. How is AI handled in an audit?

Every AI call is logged. The log captures:

When a state surveyor or LTC-insurance auditor asks "how was this care plan written?" you can show the full chain — intake notes → AI draft → supervisor edits → final approved plan, with timestamps and identities. Every record is defensible.

We also tag every AI-touched record with the model + prompt version it was generated under, so we can trace any output back to the exact AI configuration that produced it.

## Part I — Clinical Documentation & Care Plans

### Q55. How does charting work in COREcare?

Charting in COREcare is built around per-client **chart templates**. A chart template is a structured document that defines what gets recorded on every visit for that client. A template is composed of **sections**, and each section has **items** within it.

Section types include:

- Free text (general observation field)
- Meal tracking (per-meal intake percentages, hydration)
- Checkbox grid (ADLs completed, tasks performed)
- Vital signs (BP, HR, temperature, O2 saturation, weight)
- Glucose readings
- Intake / output (volumes in, volumes out — for clients on fluid restriction or kidney monitoring)
- Bowel movement (frequency, type, notes)
- Medication administration events (which med, dose, time, route, who administered)
- Nursing notes (RN-only, free-form clinical notes)

You build a template once for a client and the caregiver sees the same structured document every time they visit. The template lives alongside the care plan and references the same goals/interventions/tasks.

When a visit happens, a **chart instance** is created from the template — that's the filled-in version for that specific visit. Chart instances move through a lifecycle (draft → in-progress → finalized) and finalized charts feed both clinical reporting and the audit log.

### Q56. What clinical entry types are supported beyond ADLs?

Many home-care clients are not just companionship. They have multiple chronic conditions, post-acute recovery needs, or skilled-care components in their plan. COREcare supports clinical entries that match what real caregiver and supervisor workflows demand:

- **Vital signs**: blood pressure (systolic/diastolic), heart rate, temperature, O2 saturation, weight. Each entry validated against standard ranges; out-of-range values are flagged for supervisor review.
- **Glucose readings**: blood glucose with timing relative to meal/medication.
- **Intake / output**: liquids consumed (ounces/ml) vs. urinary output volumes — relevant for clients on fluid restriction, on diuretics, or recovering post-hospital.
- **Bowel movements**: frequency, type (Bristol scale or similar), notes — relevant for clients on bowel programs or with constipation/incontinence concerns.
- **Medication administration**: per administered dose, the medication, dose, route, time, and the caregiver who administered. Distinct from the medication master record (Q59) which is what's *prescribed*; this is what was *actually given*.
- **Nursing notes**: RN-only free-form clinical notes (visible to the assigning supervisor; not visible to non-clinical office staff or families by default).

These are first-class entry types with their own validation, their own reporting paths, and their own audit-log trails. The AI structuring path (Q48) understands and can populate them from a free-text note where appropriate.

### Q57. Per-client chart customization — what's included, required, or excluded, and why?

Every section item in a client's chart template carries an **inclusion status**:

- **Included** — appears in the chart, optional to fill in
- **Required** — appears in the chart, must be filled in to finalize the visit
- **Excluded** — does not appear at all for this client

When you mark an item as excluded, you're prompted for an **exclusion reason**:

- Not applicable to this client
- Excluded by clinical decision
- Excluded due to client condition
- Excluded by family request
- Other (with free text)

Why this matters: a client with no diabetes shouldn't see a glucose section in their chart, but the *reason* a section was removed is itself valuable clinical history. When a future supervisor asks "why don't we track glucose for Margaret?" the system has the answer with a date and an actor. When a state surveyor asks the same question, you have a defensible, contemporaneous record.

You can change inclusion status at any time. The change is versioned: the chart template that was in effect on a given visit date is preserved alongside the chart instance from that visit. Changing the template today does not retroactively rewrite yesterday's documentation.

### Q58. Health reports — family-friendly and clinical versions with staff approval

Many agencies generate periodic health summaries for families and, separately, for clinical reviewers (LTC-insurance, the client's physician, a discharging hospital). COREcare generates both versions automatically from the underlying chart instances and visit history.

- **Family-friendly version**: written for the kitchen table. Plain-language summaries of how the client has been doing, ADL completion trends, mood/behavior observations, anything noteworthy. Avoids clinical jargon and avoids exposing data the family hasn't been granted access to (per the per-client family portal config — see Q74).
- **Clinical version**: written for clinical readers. Vital signs trends with values, medication administration history, intake/output trends, nursing notes, incident summaries, AI-flagged trend observations.

Reports go through an **approval queue** before they're delivered. A staff member (typically the assigned care manager or a designated supervisor) reviews the generated report, edits if needed, and signs off. Only after sign-off does the family-version go to the family portal and the clinical-version go to its destination (download, email to the requesting clinician, or attached to a payer claim).

Reports can be generated on demand or on a schedule (weekly, monthly, on a custom interval). They can be generated in batch for a set of clients (e.g., end-of-month for all your LTC-insurance clients) — at Scale tier, batch generation can be scheduled.

The whole report-generation chain is in the audit log: who generated, who approved, who delivered, when, in what version.

### Q59. Client medication master record — how is it different from per-visit medication administration?

Two different things, both important:

- **Medication master record**: the long-running list of medications a client is prescribed. "Margaret takes lisinopril 10mg every morning, atorvastatin 20mg at bedtime, and acetaminophen 500mg as needed for pain." This is the source of truth for what's prescribed. It changes when the prescription changes (new med, dose change, discontinuation).
- **Medication administration events**: the per-visit record of what was actually given. "On Tuesday at 8:15 AM, caregiver Maria S. administered lisinopril 10mg PO."

The relationship: the master record drives what's available to administer at a visit. The administration event records what actually happened (or didn't — refusals are also recorded with reason). The two together produce both the "what is this client supposed to be on?" answer and the "did they actually take it on Tuesday?" answer.

Master record changes are tracked with effective date, source (physician order, pharmacy update, family/client report), and the staff member who entered the change. Historical master record state is preserved; you can see what the active medication list was on any past date.

### Q60. Client document repository — what gets stored where?

Every client has a document repository for files that aren't structured records: signed consents, advance directives, POA paperwork, physician orders, hospital discharge summaries, prior medical records, family-provided photos for caregiver introduction, anything else worth keeping. Each document carries:

- A type (consent / advance directive / physician order / discharge summary / other)
- An effective date
- An expiration date (where applicable, e.g., physician orders)
- The uploader and upload date
- HIPAA-safe filename handling

Documents are referenced from elsewhere in the system — care plans link to the physician order they reference, the family portal config links to the access consent on file, an incident links to any related ED discharge summary. Documents are part of the audit log too: every access (download, view, share) is recorded.

### Q61. How do care plans work in COREcare?

A care plan in COREcare has a structured shape: goals, interventions, tasks, frequencies, recurring billable services, and acknowledgments. For each client, you build (or AI-draft and edit) the care plan that defines what care looks like.

Caregivers see the relevant care plan tasks at the top of every visit in the mobile app — they don't have to remember what the plan says. Every visit's documentation maps back to the active care plan tasks, so completion data accumulates against the plan over time.

When the care plan changes (because the client's needs change, or a supervisory visit identifies an update), the new version is published. Every caregiver assigned to that client sees the updated plan on their next shift, and their acknowledgment is captured. No more "did anyone tell the caregivers we changed the protocol?"

Care plans are versioned. You can always see the history of changes and what was in effect on any given day. Care plan changes flow into the family portal as notification events (per family member access settings).

### Q62. What about field supervisors / care managers?

At around 25 active clients, most agencies stop having the owner schedule directly and a field supervisor (sometimes called a care manager) emerges. Existing tools tend to miscast this role — either giving the supervisor full admin permissions (too much) or treating them like a caregiver (too little).

COREcare has a Field Supervisor (Care Manager) role with scoped permissions. A supervisor sees their assigned caseload (specific clients) and their supervisees (specific caregivers) — not the whole agency. Within that scope, they can:

- View visit documentation and trend reports for their caseload
- Author and update care plans for their clients
- Conduct supervisory visits (a distinct visit type from caregiver visits) and document them
- Capture incidents (falls, ED visits, family complaints) with escalation paths
- Coach caregivers on documentation completeness, punctuality, client-reported satisfaction trends
- Approve health reports for their caseload

The supervisor doesn't see other caseloads, can't change agency-wide settings, and doesn't have access to billing or payroll. The role is built for accountability without overreach.

A care manager can simultaneously be a caregiver (some agencies have care managers who also clock visits). When that happens, the user has a portal chooser at login and switches roles within session — without that switch causing access leaks across roles.

### Q63. Care manager action queue — what does it surface, deterministically vs. via AI?

The care manager dashboard surfaces a priority-sorted action queue. Some actions are flagged by deterministic rules; others are flagged by AI. Both are first-class:

**Deterministic flags** (fast, explainable, every time):
- **No-show**: a shift that's started 15+ minutes ago without a clock-in.
- **Overdue care request**: a request from family or staff that's been open more than a day without action.
- **Vital threshold crossing**: e.g., systolic BP > 130 mmHg, or a delta > 10 mmHg from the recent baseline.
- **Documentation gap**: a visit ended more than X hours ago without completed documentation.
- **Credential expiring**: a credential within the supervisor's caseload expiring in the next 7 days.

**AI flags** (pattern-based, advisory, always reviewable):
- ADL decline trends across multiple visits
- Repeated medication refusals
- Mood/affect shifts mentioned in observations
- Caregiver-client compatibility concerns based on prior-visit feedback

Each action in the queue has a recommended next step and links to the underlying record (the missed shift, the open request, the vital reading). The supervisor acts directly from the queue — no need to navigate to four different screens to triage.

### Q64. How are incidents captured (falls, ED visits, complaints)?

Caregivers, supervisors, and office staff can all open an Incident from the relevant client's record. Each incident captures:

- Type (fall, ED visit, hospitalization, family complaint, behavioral incident, near-miss, other)
- Date, time, location, who was present
- A free-text description (with AI structuring help if you want it)
- Immediate actions taken
- Notification status (was the family notified? supervisor? physician?)
- Follow-up tasks with assignees and due dates

Incidents trigger an escalation flow you configure (typically: supervisor notified immediately, owner notified for severity 2+, family notified per consent settings). Every incident is in the audit log forever.

For LTC-insurance and state-survey purposes, the incident report can be exported as a single PDF on demand.

## Part J — Billing & Revenue Operations

### Q65. How does billing actually work — from visit to invoice?

The billing pipeline:

1. **Visits clocked**: each completed visit creates a billable record with the bill rate for the client and the duration.
2. **Service catalog attachments**: any add-on services (Q67) attached to a visit are added as additional billable line items with their own rates.
3. **Recurring care-plan billables**: care-plan items configured as recurring billables (Q68) generate their own line items on the schedule defined in the care plan.
4. **Aggregation**: at the end of a billing period (weekly, bi-weekly, monthly — your choice in System Settings), the system aggregates all billable items into invoices, one per payer (client, family, LTC insurer, payer entity).
5. **Review**: invoices stay in **draft** status until you review them. You can edit, recategorize, or remove line items in draft.
6. **Send**: when you're satisfied, you send. Invoices are emailed to the payer (with the appropriate version — see Q71 — for family-facing) and posted to QuickBooks. Status moves to **sent**.
7. **Payment tracking**: when payment arrives (recorded manually or auto-detected via QuickBooks reconciliation), invoice status moves to **partial** or **paid**. Outstanding balances are tracked.
8. **Corrections**: if an invoice needs correction after sending, you don't void-and-redo — you issue a revision (Q66).

Every state change is in the audit log with actor, timestamp, and reason where applicable.

### Q66. Issuing corrected invoices — how do invoice revisions work?

Real agencies issue corrected invoices regularly: the wrong rate was applied, a visit was double-billed, an authorization was missed, a service was added after the original send. Pretending invoices are immutable is fiction; pretending you can edit them silently is a compliance violation.

COREcare treats invoice revisions as a first-class workflow:

1. From the original invoice, click **Issue Revision**.
2. The system creates a revision-numbered invoice (e.g., INV-2026-0341 → INV-2026-0341-R1) tied to the original. Editing happens on the revision, not the original.
3. You make the changes — edit a line, add a service, fix a rate, change an authorization reference. You enter a required revision reason.
4. You review and send. The original invoice's status updates to **superseded by R1**. The revision becomes the active invoice.
5. The full chain is preserved: original invoice content, every revision, the reasons, who issued each, when, what changed.

Revisions are also locked against concurrent issuance: if two staff members try to revise the same invoice at the same time, the second one is told the row is locked and shown the first revision in progress.

QuickBooks integration handles revisions correctly — the revised invoice replaces the original in QuickBooks (with the relationship preserved in line-item descriptions), so your books match COREcare without manual cleanup.

### Q67. The agency-managed service catalog — what is it for?

Beyond the standard hourly visit, agencies bill for add-on services: a one-off shopping trip, a special-occasion outing, a delivery fee for picking up groceries, a setup fee for a new client, a 24-hour live-in differential, a holiday premium. Hard-coding these into the platform doesn't scale — every agency's catalog is different.

The service catalog is an agency-managed list of billable add-on services. For each catalog entry, you configure:

- An **internal name** (what your staff calls it: "live-in Sunday differential")
- A **family-facing label** (what shows up on the invoice: "Sunday premium")
- A **base price** (flat fee)
- An **optional hourly overage rate** (if the service includes some hours and bills more above)
- An **estimated hours** value (used for AI scheduling fit and for caregiver shift planning)
- A **physician-order requirement flag** (if true, the service can't be billed without an active physician order on file — see Q69)
- A **soft-retire** flag (the service is no longer offered to new clients but historical visits referencing it remain valid)

Catalog entries are versioned — when you edit a price, the change is effective from a date you choose, and historical visits keep their original price.

### Q68. Recurring billable services on care plans — how are they generated and billed?

Care plans aren't just clinical — they often include billing structure. A client's care plan might specify "weekly grocery service" or "twice-monthly transportation to physical therapy" or "daily medication reminder check-in." Each of these can be configured as a recurring billable service on the care plan.

For each recurring billable, the care plan stores:

- The catalog entry it references (so the rate updates automatically if the catalog price changes — or stays grandfathered if you choose)
- The recurrence rule (weekly on Tuesdays, monthly on the first, etc.)
- The start and end dates (or open-ended)
- Who pays (client, family, payer, LTC insurer)
- Any service authorization the recurring service is billed against

When a billing period closes, the system generates the recurring billable line items automatically. They land in the draft invoice for the appropriate payer alongside the visit-based billing. You review and send.

This means the care plan is the single source of truth for "what care happens AND what gets billed." When the care plan changes, billing follows. No more parallel spreadsheet of "what are we supposed to be billing for X this month."

### Q69. Physician orders as billing gates — what's required?

Some billable services — and some clinical interventions — require a current physician order on file. This is a state-by-state and payer-by-payer requirement, and the auditor *will* check.

In COREcare, when you configure a service catalog entry with the physician-order-required flag, the billing pipeline enforces it: that service cannot be attached to a visit, billed, or invoiced unless there is an active physician order in the client document repository (Q60) referencing the service.

When an order is about to expire, the system warns the assigning supervisor 14 days in advance. When an order expires without renewal, the service becomes unbillable until a renewed order is uploaded. The override is possible (medical exception) but logged with reason and approver.

### Q70. Pay rate vs. bill rate — why are they different and how is each tracked?

The pay rate is what the *caregiver* earns. The bill rate is what the *client/payer* is charged. These are not the same number; the difference is the agency's gross margin on that visit.

Both are first-class fields on every shift:

- **Pay rate**: pulled from the caregiver's current rate (Q37), respecting effective-dated rate changes (Q38).
- **Bill rate**: pulled from the client's current bill rate, which may vary by service category (companion, personal care, skilled), by time of day (overnight differential), or by special arrangement.

Both are recorded *at the time of the shift* — historical accuracy. If you raise pay rates on April 1, visits before April 1 keep the old pay rate; visits on/after use the new rate. Same for bill rates.

This dual-rate model is what makes gross-margin reporting work. You can answer: "what did our companion-care visits gross-margin at last month?" Without separating pay and bill rate, agencies typically have to derive this offline in a spreadsheet — and then they don't.

### Q71. Statement generation, family-facing invoices, and payment tracking

Many private-pay clients receive invoices addressed to a family member or POA. Many LTC-insurance claims require very specific invoice formats. COREcare handles both:

- **Family-facing invoice**: a clean, plain-English statement showing the period, the visits with caregiver names and durations, any add-on services, and the amount due. Pricing detail can be configured per-client (some families prefer hour-by-hour detail; others prefer summary lines).
- **Payer-facing invoice**: a structured invoice that conforms to the payer's required format (most LTC insurers have specific field requirements). Configurable per payer in System Settings.
- **Statement generation**: monthly or on-demand statements aggregating all activity for a payer, including invoices issued, payments received, and outstanding balance.
- **Payment tracking**: payments are recorded against invoices manually or auto-applied via QuickBooks reconciliation. Aging reports show what's outstanding by client, by payer, by period.

### Q72. Payment processors and accepted methods

For private-pay billing where you want to collect electronically, COREcare integrates with payment processors so families can pay invoices online via ACH or card. The integration is opt-in and routes through your QuickBooks Payments setup (or, on the roadmap, a direct Stripe integration for agencies that don't use QuickBooks Payments).

Payments received via the integration are auto-applied to the matching invoice. Payments received outside the integration (mailed checks, in-person payments, payer-side EFTs) are entered manually and the same auto-apply logic runs against your AR.

### Q73. What do families see?

By default, an approved family member with portal access sees:

You can configure per-client what families see. Some families are very involved and want everything; others are observers; some clients prefer to keep most details private. The portal respects per-client consent settings.

### Q74. How do I control what families see for which clients?

Each client has a Family Portal configuration. You set, per client:

### Q75. Do families pay extra? (No.)

No. Family portal seats are unlimited at every tier. We don't charge per family member. The family is part of the care team in our model — paywalling family access would be weird and counterproductive.

### Q76. Can families message us?

Yes, securely, through the portal. Family-to-agency messages are logged. Messages are PHI-safe (the system flags PHI in outbound messages and offers safer alternatives). You can route family messages by topic if you want — billing questions to one queue, care concerns to another.

### Q77. Family invite codes — how do families self-register?

Adding a new family member to a client doesn't require an admin to type in their email and send a manual invitation every time. Instead, you generate a **family invite code** for the client. The code is a single-use, time-limited string. You hand it (or text it, or print it on the welcome packet) to the family member.

The family member goes to the family portal sign-up page, enters the code, and creates their account. The code identifies which client they're being granted access to and pre-populates their access level per the client's Family Portal configuration. After they register, the code is consumed and can't be reused.

You can revoke a code before it's used. You can revoke the access after it's been used. Both actions are logged.

This pattern means a family member can sign themselves up out-of-hours, on a Sunday afternoon, without your office staff in the loop — which is when families typically have time to deal with this stuff.

### Q80. What about SOC 2?

SOC 2 Type II audit is in progress. Our target completion is within 6–12 months of v1 GA.

Until the formal SOC 2 report is available, we publish our security posture, control set, and roadmap to SOC 2 in a security overview document available on request. For agencies serving enterprise-adjacent clients (large LTC insurers, hospital partnerships), this gives you what you need for diligence in the interim.

### Q81. How is my data isolated from other agencies' data?

Two layers.

The first is application-layer access control: every API request is checked against your tenant identity, and only your agency's data is returned.

The second — and the one that matters more — is database-layer isolation. We use **row-level security (RLS)** in our database, which means a single misconfigured permission in the application layer cannot leak another agency's data. The database itself enforces tenant boundaries on every query. This is materially stronger than application-only isolation and is uncommon in this category.

Practically: even if a future bug in our application code attempted to query across tenants, the database would reject the request. Your data and your neighbor's data live in the same physical database for cost reasons, but they are cryptographically and policy-segregated at the storage layer.

The one exception is COREcare staff super-admin access for support — see Q89, which is intentional, audited, and visible to you.

### Q82. EVV — do you handle it?

We are **not an EVV system**. We are a back-office care operations platform that integrates with EVV systems.

If you serve HCBS waiver clients in a state with mandatory EVV, you'll have an existing EVV aggregator (HHAeXchange, Sandata, or your state-built system). COREcare captures verified visits in our own (non-GPS) verification framework and pushes that verified-visit data into your EVV aggregator via API.

What this means in practice: your caregivers continue to use the COREcare app. COREcare automatically forwards the EVV-required fields (caregiver ID, client ID, visit start/end, location verification, services delivered) to your aggregator. You don't operate two systems. The EVV system stays the system of record for state Medicaid; COREcare stays the system of record for everything else.

For Medicare home health agencies needing OASIS-E2 EVV: we don't serve that segment.

### Q83. State Medicaid / HCBS waiver work — what's supported?

We support agencies that do some HCBS waiver work alongside their primary private-pay or LTC-insurance business, as long as the EVV requirement is satisfied by a separate state aggregator (per Q82).

What we do *not* do:

- We are not an EVV system ourselves
- We don't bill state Medicaid directly (we provide the visit data; your billing system or your claims clearinghouse handles the submission)
- We don't manage state-specific waiver authorization workflows beyond service authorization tracking and visit-against-authorization reporting

If your business is predominantly Medicaid HCBS waiver work, HHAeXchange or a state-aggregator-anchored stack is probably a better fit.

### Q84. The audit log — what's actually in it?

Every PHI access, every change to a client record, every visit modification, every care plan version change, every credential update, every AI invocation, every login, every permission change, every system-setting change, every billing edit, every payroll edit, every vendor support session — captured with timestamp, actor identity, action, and (where applicable) before/after values.

The audit log is queryable through the admin dashboard at Growth and Scale tiers, with filters by user, date range, client, action type, and severity. Starter tier gets a read-only view of the same log; Scale gets export.

Two design principles: the audit log is not just for the back office — it's a customer feature. And it's append-only at the storage layer; nobody (including us) can delete or modify entries. See Q86.

### Q85. State surveys and LTC-insurance audits — how does COREcare help?

State surveyors and LTC-insurance auditors typically ask three things:

1. **Show me the care plan and prove it was followed.** Care plans are versioned with a published date; every visit's documentation maps to the care plan tasks active that day. You produce a single report showing care plan adherence over the audit window.
2. **Show me caregiver credentials were current at the time of service.** The credential system tracks expirations historically. You can produce a report showing every credential's status on every shift date.
3. **Show me your access controls and audit trail.** The audit log is exportable (Scale tier) or queryable (Growth tier) for the audit window.

What used to be a three-week scramble across spreadsheets and paper files becomes three reports you generate from the dashboard. Most agency owners we've talked to have described their last audit as the worst week of their year. We want this to be the thing about COREcare you don't think about until you need it.

### Q86. Append-only logs — what does that mean for my data and your operators?

Append-only means: once an entry is written to the audit log, neither you, nor your staff, nor COREcare engineers, nor COREcare super-admins can modify or delete it. The storage layer enforces it — there is no API call, no admin action, no support request that can rewrite history.

This matters for two reasons:

- **For you**: when an audit happens and somebody asks "did anyone change the care plan after the fact?" the answer is verifiable. The audit log is the evidence.
- **For us**: when something goes wrong (a vendor support session goes off-script, a contractor is asked to do something they shouldn't), the audit log catches it. Our incident response runbook starts with the audit log; if the log shows the incident, we have a fix path. If the log is somehow missing a fingerprint we expected, that's itself an incident.

The append-only pattern is uncomfortable for vendors who like to "fix data" silently. It's a feature for vendors and customers who want defensibility.

## Part M — Customer Support & Vendor Access

### Q87. How does vendor support actually work?

COREcare is operated by the same team that builds it. We don't outsource first-tier support. Every ticket is answered by a COREcare team member who uses the product. When you open a chat or call, you're not talking to someone reading a script in a different timezone with no access to your data — you're talking to an engineer who can look at exactly what happened, find the bug, and fix it (or escalate it to whoever can).

Tier-by-tier response commitments are in Q109. The substantive thing this section covers: *what a support engineer can see and do inside your tenant*, and how that's controlled.

### Q88. Staff impersonation (View As) — how does it work and who can use it?

Many support questions are unanswerable without seeing the user's screen. "It says my hours are wrong" is impossible to investigate from text alone. Asking the user to screen-share over Zoom is a friction barrier most caregivers won't cross.

COREcare's impersonation surface — internally called **View As** — solves this. There are two flavors:

**Agency-internal impersonation.** Your admin staff can View As a caregiver, family member, or care manager *within your own tenant*. Use cases: a scheduler debugging "why doesn't this caregiver see the shift I assigned them?", an admin testing a new care manager's permissions, a supervisor confirming what the family portal looks like for a specific family member. Every page viewed under impersonation is logged with the actor identity, the impersonated identity, and the page. Impersonation cannot perform certain forbidden actions (changing the impersonated user's password, signing the impersonated user's e-consent forms, sending messages as the impersonated user) — those are blocked at the platform level and flagged in the audit log if attempted.

**Vendor impersonation.** COREcare support engineers can, with your explicit consent, View As a user inside your tenant for a limited time window. See Q89.

In both cases, the impersonation banner is permanently visible at the top of the screen during the session, identifying who is impersonating whom. You always know when impersonation is active.

### Q89. Vendor super-admin access — when, why, and with what audit trail?

Some support questions require COREcare to look at your data — not from your admin's perspective (View As), but from a higher-privileged vendor perspective. Examples: investigating a billing calculation that involves cross-tenant logic; debugging an integration that touches your payroll provider; running an emergency data correction during an incident; investigating a security alert.

We've designed this carefully because it's the most sensitive capability in the platform.

**How it works:**

- Only specific COREcare roles (engineering on-call, security incident response, designated support engineers) have super-admin capability. The list is published.
- Super-admin access into a tenant requires either (a) a customer-initiated support ticket where you've explicitly authorized the engineer to look, or (b) an active security incident where pre-authorized incident response procedures apply (your BAA covers this).
- Every super-admin session into your tenant is recorded: the engineer's identity, the start time, the end time, every record accessed, every action taken. The session record is visible to you in your tenant's vendor-access log (see Q90).
- Super-admin sessions are time-bounded. Default is 4 hours; longer requires re-authorization.
- Super-admin access cannot bypass the append-only audit log — nothing is written silently. If a COREcare engineer takes an action in your tenant, that action is in your audit log under their identity, with the super-admin context tag.

The platform is designed so that super-admin access is *transparent*, *auditable*, and *finite*. The pattern most vendors use — quietly elevated permissions held forever — is not what we do.

### Q90. How do I review or revoke vendor access into my tenant?

Your admin dashboard has a **Vendor Access** view that shows:

- Every active vendor super-admin session into your tenant (right now)
- Every past session in the audit window, with the engineer, the time bounds, the related ticket, and a summary of actions taken
- Pending consent requests (a COREcare engineer has requested access for a ticket; you haven't yet granted)

From this view you can:

- **Approve or deny** a pending consent request
- **Revoke** an active session (the engineer's session ends immediately; the audit-log entries already written stay)
- **Configure** standing policy: do you want the platform to require explicit per-incident consent every time, or do you pre-authorize incident-response access under your BAA, or do you want notification-only without blocking? (The default is per-incident consent for support, pre-authorized for security incidents under BAA.)

Vendor access without your awareness is not possible. Every super-admin entry into your tenant produces a notification (in-app + email to your admins) and creates an entry in the Vendor Access view.

## Part N — Operational Reliability

### Q91. The in-app notification inbox — what's in it?

Every user (admin, scheduler, caregiver, care manager, family) has an in-app notification inbox. This is the single triage surface for things the user needs to know about, separate from email and SMS (which can fall through the cracks).

What lands in the inbox depends on the user's role:

- **Admins**: credential expirations, payroll period ready for review, pending approvals (expenses, rate changes, etc.), system setting changes, vendor access notifications, alerts from the deterministic action queue
- **Schedulers**: open shifts, call-outs, swap requests awaiting approval
- **Caregivers**: new shifts assigned, shift updates, swap responses, expense status updates, credential expiration alerts, message replies
- **Care managers**: action queue alerts (no-shows, overdue requests, vital threshold crossings), care plan acknowledgments, incident reports

### Q92. Email and SMS reliability — what guarantees do you make?

Email and SMS reliability is a real operational risk in home care — credential alerts and shift offers depend on it. COREcare instruments delivery aggressively:

- Every email and SMS we send is logged in the system: recipient, subject, status (queued / sent / delivered / bounced / opened / clicked), error codes for failures.
- We run periodic delivery-canary tests across our sending domains and number pools. If our delivery rate drops below baseline, we know within minutes and route around the failing path (different sending domain, different SMS pool).
- We use SendGrid and Twilio under the hood today (subject to change). We sign BAAs with both. We don't send PHI in email subject lines or message bodies; the email is a notification that a message exists, with a link to the secure portal where the actual content lives.

We don't publish a per-message SLA (those are typically spurious — the deliverability problem isn't usually our outbound infra; it's the recipient's spam filter). What we do publish: monthly delivery-rate ranges in our annual cost-savings disclosure (Q9), so you can hold us accountable to the actual numbers.

### Q93. Configurable system settings — what's tunable, and is the change audited?

A lot of agency-specific behavior is controlled by system settings, including:

- Mileage rate (defaults to current IRS rate; you can override)
- Overtime threshold (per state; defaults to your state's standard; can be more generous)
- Family billing default (how invoices are split between family and other payers when both apply)
- Holiday calendar (what dates trigger holiday pay)
- Meal/rest break rules (per state; see Q40)
- Pay period schedule (weekly / bi-weekly / semi-monthly / monthly)
- Approval thresholds for expenses
- Notification volume defaults per role
- Time zone for the agency

Every setting change is captured in the audit log: who changed it, when, from what value to what value, with a required change reason. Some settings — those with payroll or billing implications — additionally use a **confirmable change** flow: you make the change, the system computes a preview of the impact (e.g., "this overtime threshold change will affect 47 shifts in the current pay period; estimated cost change: +$1,240"), and you confirm or cancel before the change applies.

This makes "I changed one setting last Tuesday and now my payroll is off by thousands" a recoverable mistake, not a Tuesday-evening crisis.

### Q94. Status page and DB-down resilience

### Q95. Rate limiting, abuse prevention, and account safety

The platform applies rate limits on critical endpoints — clock-in, login, charting submission, AI invocation, expense submission — to defeat fat-finger floods and credential stuffing. Limits are per-user and per-IP.

If a user trips a rate limit, they see a clear error message (not a generic "too many requests") and a recommended next action. Rate-limit violations from the same actor across a window trigger account-safety reviews.

For login specifically:
- Failed-login attempts are throttled, then locked, then require step-up verification
- We monitor for credential-stuffing patterns (high-volume failed logins across many accounts from the same source) and block sources that match
- Caregivers can self-recover via the magic-link flow (Q97); admins must use password reset, which we've intentionally made non-magic to prevent escalation via compromised email

## Part O — Authentication, Identity & Access Control

### Q96. How do users log in?

By role:

- **Caregivers and family members** can log in via password or magic link (one-tap login from a time-limited link sent to their verified email or SMS). Magic link is the typical pattern; passwords are available for caregivers who prefer them.
- **Admins, schedulers, care managers** log in via password, with optional second-factor (TOTP-based authenticator app or hardware key). Second-factor is strongly recommended; for Scale-tier and Enterprise customers it can be required at the tenant level.
- **SSO** (SAML / OIDC / OAuth-based identity providers like Okta, Google Workspace, Microsoft Entra) is on the roadmap for v1.x; available at Growth and Scale.

Sessions expire by default after a period of idle activity (Q99), with the period configurable per role.

### Q97. Magic links, passwordless, and why staff can't use them

Magic links are a compelling UX for low-friction, infrequent users — caregivers checking the schedule, families checking visit summaries. We support them for those audiences with these properties:

- One-time use (the link is consumed when followed)
- Time-bounded (expires in 30 minutes for caregiver/admin, 4 hours for family — calibrated to typical use cadence)
- Tenant-scoped and role-scoped (a magic link issued for one tenant can't be redeemed against another)
- Audit-logged (every issue, every redemption, every failure)

**Magic links are explicitly disabled for staff (admin, scheduler, care manager) accounts.** A magic link grants the holder full access to whatever the underlying account can do — an OK pattern for a caregiver checking their schedule, an unacceptable pattern for an admin who can change pay rates, edit invoices, or access the full audit log. Staff accounts use passwords (preferably with 2FA). This is a deliberate security stance, not an oversight.

### Q98. Granular capabilities — beyond simple roles

Most platforms in this category have coarse roles: "admin," "office user," "caregiver," "family." Coarse roles force you to grant more access than necessary because the next-step-down doesn't exist.

COREcare uses role-based access control (RBAC) plus a layer of granular capabilities. A capability is a specific permission like "attach a billable service to a visit," "approve a payroll period," "edit a finalized chart," "issue an invoice revision," or "approve a family invite code." Capabilities are bundled into roles by default (the standard "admin" role has all the relevant capabilities), but you can:

- Create custom roles that bundle specific capabilities
- Grant individual capabilities to a user without changing their primary role
- Audit-log the grant of any capability to any user

This is what lets you, for example, give a billing clerk the "issue invoice revision" capability and the "attach billable service" capability without granting them payroll edit, access to the full audit log, or system-setting changes.

The capability set is documented at developer.corecare.com.

### Q99. Sessions, idle timeout, and step-up authentication for sensitive actions

Idle timeouts are configurable per role and default to:

- Admin / scheduler / care manager: 30 minutes idle
- Caregiver: 4 hours idle (caregiver shifts run long; constant re-auth on the phone is hostile)
- Family: 60 minutes idle

Beyond timeouts, certain actions trigger **step-up authentication** — the user is prompted to re-enter their password or 2FA token even within an active session. Examples of step-up actions:

- Approving a payroll period
- Issuing an invoice revision over a configurable amount
- Granting vendor super-admin access
- Editing the system setting "magic link disabled for staff" (you can't even change this without confirming you're you)
- Granting/revoking another user's capabilities

Step-up failures count toward rate limiting and lockout policy, just like login failures.

## Part P — Architecture & Integrations

### Q100. How is COREcare architected, and why does it matter to me as a customer?

COREcare is built as a network of small services connected by stable internal APIs. Practically, that means:

- **Services own their data.** The scheduling service owns the schedule. The billing service owns invoices. The clinical service owns chart instances. They communicate by API, not by reaching into each other's databases.
- **Services can be deployed and scaled independently.** A clinical-charting bug fix doesn't require redeploying the scheduling service. A heavy AI workload can be sized differently from the lightweight read traffic.
- **Internal API contracts are versioned.** Breaking changes go through a deprecation window, never a silent rip-and-replace. Integrations don't break when we move things around internally because the contracts are stable.

Why this matters to you as a customer:

- **Faster fixes.** When something breaks, the blast radius is bounded. We can ship a fix to the affected service without staging a full-platform release.
- **Predictable extensibility.** When you build on top of our public API (Q106), you're using the same contracts our internal services use. Your integration won't break because we shipped a feature you don't use.
- **Operational transparency.** Each service has its own health metric in our status page (Q94). When something is degraded, you see exactly which capability is affected, not a generic "service degraded."

We're not selling architecture. But the architecture is downstream of the customer commitments — small services with stable contracts is how we deliver "fast fixes," "no surprise integration breakage," and "transparent operational state" without that becoming aspiration.

### Q101. Internal API contracts — what does that get me as an integrator?

The same contracts our services use to talk to each other are exposed as our public API (with appropriate authentication and tenant scoping). When we say the public API is feature-complete with the web app, we mean it: there's no "platform behavior we never document." If the COREcare web app can do something, the API can do the same thing.

This matters when you build:

- A custom dashboard that pulls billing, scheduling, and clinical data into one BI view
- An integration with a payer portal that posts visit data on a schedule
- A workflow automation that watches for credential expirations and emails the caregiver's preferred contact
- A bespoke caregiver-facing app that uses our backend (Enterprise tier; requires explicit approval)

The contracts are versioned. v1 of the public API has deprecation windows of 12 months; we don't break customer integrations on a quarterly release cadence.

### Q102. QuickBooks

We integrate natively with QuickBooks Online. The integration handles:

- Pushing caregiver hours and pay-period totals as payroll entries
- Pushing client billing data as invoice line items
- Pushing invoice revisions correctly (the revised invoice replaces the original; the relationship is preserved in line-item descriptions)
- Pushing approved expense reimbursements as appropriate ledger entries
- Mapping COREcare service categories to your QuickBooks chart of accounts (you configure once)
- Refreshing nightly, with manual sync-on-demand

QuickBooks Desktop is supported via export-to-IIF for now; native API integration is on the roadmap.

### Q103. Payroll processors (Gusto, Rippling, ADP, Paychex)

Direct integrations with Gusto and Rippling are available at Growth and Scale tiers. The integration pushes caregiver pay-period hours, mileage pay, expense reimbursements, and consented overpayment deductions directly to your payroll provider, eliminating manual transcription.

ADP and Paychex direct integrations are on the v1.x roadmap. In the interim, COREcare exports a payroll-ready CSV in a configurable format that those providers can ingest.

### Q104. Earned wage access (DailyPay, Tapcheck)

Optional integrations with DailyPay and Tapcheck let your caregivers access earned wages between pay periods, surfaced in the COREcare app. This is a meaningful caregiver retention feature — earned-wage-access has been shown to materially reduce turnover at agencies that offer it.

You sign your own EWA contract directly with the provider; COREcare connects the visit-hour data so the EWA balance is accurate in real time.

### Q105. SMS notifications

Built in at every tier with an included monthly volume (500 / 2,000 / 5,000 messages per Starter / Growth / Scale). Used for:

- Caregiver shift reminders and call-out broadcasts
- Schedule change notifications to caregivers and clients
- Credential expiration alerts (in addition to in-app inbox and email)
- Family-portal notifications (if family member opted into SMS)
- Magic link delivery (where SMS-based magic links are configured)

If you exceed the included volume, we charge $0.02 / $0.015 / $0.01 per message at Starter / Growth / Scale. Your dashboard shows real-time SMS volume so you can predict cost.

### Q106. Public API access — what can I do with it?

At Starter, the API is read-only — useful for pulling data into your own reporting or business intelligence tools.

At Growth, the API is full read/write. You can build custom integrations (for example, into a payer portal, into your own custom dashboard, into a marketing automation system, into a BI tool like Power BI or Tableau).

At Scale, you also get webhooks (Q107) — the system can push events to your endpoints in real time.

API documentation is published at developer.corecare.com (live alongside v1 GA).

### Q107. Webhooks and event streams

Available at Scale tier. You register endpoints to receive event notifications in real time:

- Visit completed
- Visit documentation finalized
- Credential expiring (30/14/7-day warnings + day-of)
- Incident captured
- Schedule published
- Invoice issued / revised / paid
- Expense submitted / approved / reimbursed
- Care plan version published
- AI flag raised in care manager action queue
- System setting changed

Each event payload includes a versioned schema. Webhooks are delivered with retries on failure (5 attempts over 30 minutes, exponential backoff). Failed deliveries land in a dead-letter queue that you can inspect and re-drive.

This is what you'd use for, say, an integration with a real-time fraud-monitoring system, a custom escalation workflow, or a third-party notification platform.

### Q108. Custom integrations and extension points

Beyond the public API and webhooks, Scale and Enterprise customers have access to a few extension points:

- **Custom report templates** that run against your tenant on a schedule and email the output to specified recipients
- **Custom dashboards** built using our reporting layer (Growth and Scale)
- **Outbound integrations** authored by you against our API (any tier with API access)
- **Inbound integrations** from third-party systems that push data to us (limited at Growth; more flexibility at Scale)

For deeply custom needs (a bespoke white-label experience, a specialized clinical module not on our roadmap), Enterprise tier conversations are how we scope that.

### Q110. What happens if your software goes down? (SLA)

We commit to 99.9% monthly uptime as a published SLA on the pricing page.

If we go below 99.9% in a given month, you receive an automatic monthly credit: 3% of your monthly fee for every additional hour of downtime above the 99.9% threshold, up to 100% of your monthly fee.

The credit is automatic — you don't have to file a claim. We compute it from our own monitoring and apply it to your next invoice. By Year 2 we publish a public uptime dashboard so you don't have to take our word for it.

### Q111. How do I cancel?

One click in your admin dashboard. Confirmation by email. Final invoice issued the same day.

We do not require a retention call. We do not have a "win-back" team that will call you. If you decide to cancel, we want it to be easy.

If you're on a month-to-month contract, no further action is needed. If you're on annual, you get a prorated refund of unused months minus the 10% annual discount.

### Q112. What happens to my data if I cancel?

Your data is **yours**. Two principles, both published on the pricing page:

1. **One-click export, anytime.** From your admin dashboard, you can export everything — client records, caregiver records, schedules, visit documentation, care plans, audit log, billing history, expense history, payroll history — as CSV or JSON. No fees. Available the day you sign up, available the day you cancel, available between.
2. **90-day post-cancellation grace.** For 90 days after cancellation, your data remains available for export and you can re-activate without any data loss. After 90 days, your data is permanently deleted from active systems (and from backups within an additional 60 days), unless you've explicitly requested longer retention or are subject to an active legal hold.

We do not charge for data export. We do not require lawyers to retrieve your business records. We do not "lose" your export request.

### Q113. Self-serve migration *away* from COREcare — is it as easy as migrating in?

We hold ourselves to the same standard for migration *out* as migration *in*.

Concretely:

- **Export to common formats** (CSV, JSON) at any time, no fees, full record set.
- **Export to specific competitors** in their native import format where we can. We've prioritized export adapters for AxisCare, Alora (where the LivTech-acquired entity continues to accept imports), and AlayaCare, because these are the systems agencies most often consider after us. More coming.
- **Schema documentation** for every export at developer.corecare.com so your destination's IT team or migration vendor knows exactly what they're getting.
- **No artificial barriers**: no "export only to PDF," no "fields the customer has paid for don't export," no "run the export from 9-to-5 only."
- **One-click bulk media export**: receipts, document repository entries, photo verifications, signed consents — all bundled into a single archive for download or for transfer to a destination of your choice.

We want you to stay because the product earns it. We don't want you to stay because we made leaving expensive.
