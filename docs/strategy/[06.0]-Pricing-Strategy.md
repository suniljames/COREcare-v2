## 1. Executive Summary

### 1.1. Headline Recommendation

> **COREcare v2 ships with three published tiers — Starter $349/mo, Growth $749/mo, Scale $1,499/mo — priced per active client served (not per caregiver seat), with unlimited staff seats, family portal, and every AI feature included at every tier. Overage pricing is published and capped: $12, $10, $8 per additional active client. We commit publicly to never raising prices on existing customers and to publishing an annual cost-savings disclosure each January documenting where AI/infra savings went — features, price drops, or margin.**

This recommendation **replaces MRD §6.3** in three specific ways:
1. The per-caregiver-seat option floated in the Pro tier is **eliminated**. It contradicts the lane thesis and arms incumbents with a per-seat-floor argument we don't need to give them.
2. Tier prices are **specified to a single number** (not a range) so the published page is unambiguous and the competitive signal is sharp.
3. A **trust-architecture commitment** (never-raise + annual disclosure) is added as a load-bearing pricing-page element, not a footnote.

### 1.2. Strategic Posture

This pricing executes an **aggressive-credible** posture in a market where the dominant competitive failure mode is per-seat pricing on a high-turnover workforce (the 79% caregiver turnover documented in [01]). Unit economics for incumbents quietly collapse when a 30-staff agency has 90 W-2s churn through the system in a year — they're paying for seats that turned over four months ago. COREcare's per-active-client unit eliminates the customer's most acute pricing pain and converts our cost structure (one-person, AI-leveraged, modern stack) into a permanent margin advantage that incumbents on per-seat models cannot match without burning their own ARR.

The "unlimited seats, free family portal, every AI feature included" framing is the marketing weapon, not a feature list. Every line of the pricing page is calibrated to make a private-duty SMB owner ask one question on their AxisCare/AlayaCare renewal call: *"why am I paying per seat for a workforce that turns over once a year?"*

### 1.3. The Bezos Frame Applied

Jeff Bezos's "your margin is my opportunity" observation maps cleanly onto this category. Incumbent gross margins are propped up by:
- **Per-seat pricing on a 79%-turnover workforce** — the single largest hidden margin pool in the category
- **Custom-quote opacity** that lets sales reps anchor SMB owners to enterprise prices
- **Module unbundling** that turns a $500/mo entry quote into a $1,500/mo all-in invoice
- **Multi-year contract lock-in** that suppresses competitive shopping

COREcare's cost structure — fully-loaded OpEx **15–30% of incumbent OpEx per customer `[EST]`** (see §5) — lets us collapse all four margin pools and still ship 70%+ gross margin at scale. We are not running a price war for its own sake. We are systematically removing the structural moats incumbents built on customer pain and replacing them with structural moats built on transparency, AI cost-efficiency, and the trust earned by never raising a published price.

### 1.5. The Trust Architecture (the most contrarian piece)

Two commitments, published on the pricing page, that no competitor in the category will match:

**Commitment 1 — Never-Raise Pricing on Existing Customers.** Every customer's price is locked at signup, forever. We can change list pricing for new customers; we cannot raise yours. This is published, not whispered in a sales call. It is the single most powerful trust signal available to a vertical SaaS startup attacking a category where the documented pattern is annual increases (Toast 5–10%/yr, Gusto 23% Simple-tier increase March 2026 [C], ServiceTitan opaque escalators).

**Commitment 2 — Annual Cost-Savings Disclosure.** Every January, COREcare publishes a one-page disclosure documenting (a) what our per-customer infrastructure cost was the prior year, (b) what it is the current year, (c) what we did with the delta — features shipped, price drops issued, or margin retained. This is asymmetric trust architecture: it costs us almost nothing operationally (one PDF a year) and it converts the Bezos thesis from a marketing line into a verifiable, repeating proof point.

This is **not** a hard pass-through commitment (option A in our pre-write deliberation). A hard pass-through is operationally suicidal pre-Series A. The asymmetric version preserves margin optionality while building the same flywheel. By Year 3, the cumulative disclosure record itself becomes a competitive moat — incumbents can't credibly copy it without exposing their own opaque pricing histories.

#### AxisCare `[C]`

  1. **Custom-quote** at sub-$500/mo price points is a poor product/motion fit — the inside-sales cost of a $200/mo deal collapses LTV/CAC.

  3. **2024 security incident transparency** flagged in customer reviews (§4) — not a pricing failure but an audit-architecture failure that compounds price-trust erosion.
- **COREcare wedge:** Match the ~$200 floor at $349 with **vastly more value visibility** (published page, no quote-to-quote variance, family portal included). AxisCare's offensive posture means we are not going to out-feature them quickly — we have to out-trust them.

#### AlayaCare `[C]`

The pricing page can say in one sentence what AlayaCare's takes a discovery call to clarify. Undercut by 50–65% and the value-density delta closes the deal.

### 2.3. Pattern Synthesis (Narrow Market)

Three patterns dominate the narrow market:

1. **Custom-quote opacity is the modal pattern** (7 of 10 vendors). Aaniie, ShiftCare, and Rosemark are the only published-pricing players. The category has trained SMB owners to expect a discovery call and then be embarrassed by the quote — which is exactly the friction COREcare's published-pricing posture monetizes.

2. **Per-seat / per-user pricing is structurally common** (4 of 10 vendors hit it somewhere in their stack: ShiftCare, Generations, Rosemark upper tiers, AxisCare for some configurations). On a workforce with 79% annual turnover, this is the single largest hidden margin pool incumbents extract from customers — and the single most attackable surface in the category.

3. **AI is moving from add-on to baseline** but no vendor has yet committed to "AI included at every tier." AxisCare Intelligence and AlayaCare Layla are add-ons or premium-tier-only [C]. CareSmartz, Rosemark, Generations have no AI story. Aaniie includes AI on Pro/Enterprise but not the $195 entry SKU. The "AI included at every tier" position is **literally unoccupied in the lane as of analysis date** and is COREcare's cleanest single-feature pricing differentiator.

## 3. Broad-Market Pricing Learnings

This section extracts transferable lessons from ten broad-market vertical SaaS, infrastructure, and developer-tools exemplars. The anchor argument from [03] — that COREcare is a vertical SaaS archetype, not a health-tech play — is operationalized here. Every lesson is filtered through the ICP and ARPA constraints of the COREcare lane.

### 3.1. Comparison Table

| Company | Category | Pricing Model | Model Lesson | Anti-Pattern Lesson |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **ServiceTitan** | Field service vertical SaaS | Per-tech/mo + heavy implementation | Vertical SaaS justifies premium ARPA when ROI is operationally legible | Per-seat at scale = customer hostility; data-export friction = brand damage |
| **Toast** | Restaurant vertical SaaS | $0/mo Starter + processing fees | Bundle infrastructure; let one cost line carry margin | "Sticker price ≠ real price" complaints are a lifetime brand cost |
| **Jobber** | SMB field service | Tiered + per-extra-user + add-on modules | Transparent published pricing is itself a moat | À la carte add-on stacking creates "module fatigue" identical to AlayaCare |
| **Gusto** | SMB payroll vertical SaaS | Base + per-employee tiered | Clean SMB model; published pricing | Annual price increases erode trust (23% Simple-tier hike March 2026) |
| **Linear** | Dev tools | Per-user tiered, transparent | Pricing-page design is a brand surface | Per-seat is per-seat — same workforce-turnover failure mode applies in dev tools |
| **Anthropic API** | LLM infrastructure | Per-MTok input/output, batch/cache discounts | Pure Bezos: capability up, price flat — directly maps to never-raise pledge | None for our purposes — model is what we're emulating |
| **Stripe** | Payments infrastructure | Per-transaction + flat | Transparent published pricing on infrastructure that scales | Surcharge stacking on international/dispute fees has been criticized |
| **Vercel** | Frontend infrastructure | Tiered + usage overage | "Free tier into paid" funnel works; published overage is acceptable | Usage spikes on Hobby/Pro that surprise customers = trust damage |
| **Datadog** | Observability | Per-host/mo + per-feature add-ons | Per-host on infrastructure that customers control is honest | Add-on stacking creates surprise invoices — same module fatigue pattern |
| **Notion** | Knowledge management | Per-user free → paid tiers | Generous free tier seeds bottom-up adoption | AI add-on at $10/user that wasn't included triggered customer complaints in 2023–24 |

### 3.2. Lessons Extracted

#### Lesson 1 — Vertical SaaS justifies premium ARPA when ROI is operationally legible (ServiceTitan)

ServiceTitan charges $245–$398/tech/mo plus $5K–$50K implementation `[C]` because the platform demonstrably saves operational hours per technician per week. The lesson is **not** "charge more"; it is "the ARPA ceiling in a vertical is set by operational ROI legibility, not by what feels cheap or expensive in absolute dollars." For COREcare, the operational ROI surfaces are: caregiver retention improvement (every 1% retention improvement is documented dollar value), shift-fill time reduction, and family-portal-driven satisfaction lift. **Implication:** the recommended Growth tier at $749/mo is defensible if the ROI story is told in retention-percentage-points, not in software-features.

#### Lesson 2 — Per-seat at scale is customer hostility (ServiceTitan anti-pattern)

ServiceTitan's per-tech pricing has generated documented hostility: BBB complaints about data-export friction post-cancellation, lawyer involvement to retrieve customer business records, contract escalator opacity. The pattern: per-seat pricing on a workforce that scales creates a hostile cost trajectory that the customer experiences as the vendor profiting from the customer's growth. **Implication:** COREcare's per-active-client unit is the inverse — when an agency grows from 25 to 75 active clients, COREcare's bill grows from $349 to ~$840/mo. That feels like the vendor sharing in growth, not extracting from it.

#### Lesson 3 — Bundle and let one cost line carry margin (Toast)

Toast offers a $0/mo Starter SKU and lets payment processing fees do the heavy lifting on margin. The customer perceives "free POS." The vendor captures economics through a different mechanism. The lesson is **not** to mimic Toast's processing-fee structure (COREcare doesn't process payments) but to recognize that **bundling visible-value items at $0 incremental cost** lets the headline tier price feel like a steal. **Implication:** family portal seats at $0, staff seats at $0, AI features at $0-incremental-cost are not loss leaders — they are bundled-into-base value that customers see immediately and competitors have to charge for.

#### Lesson 4 — Annual price increases erode trust faster than they help margin (Gusto anti-pattern)

Gusto raised the Simple plan price 23% in March 2026 `[C]`. Customer complaints have been visible. ServiceTitan, Toast, and most SaaS incumbents run 5–10% annual price escalators. The cumulative effect on trust is large — multi-year customers feel the vendor breaking the implicit deal. **Implication:** the never-raise pledge for existing customers is asymmetrically valuable. We give up the option to raise prices on a customer base that grows slowly anyway (single-digit-percent customer growth × 5–10% annual increase = small marginal revenue) in exchange for a trust signal competitors structurally cannot match.

#### Lesson 5 — Pure Bezos: capability up, price flat (Anthropic API)

Anthropic's pricing for the Claude 4 model family — Haiku 4.5 at $1/$5 per MTok, Sonnet 4.6 at $3/$15, Opus 4.7 at $5/$25 `[C]` — has held flat through generation upgrades despite measurable capability uplift. Batch processing at 50% off and prompt caching at 90% off are infrastructure-level price reductions delivered to customers `[C]`. **Implication:** this is the operational template for the COREcare annual disclosure. We document our infra cost per customer, document that it dropped, document where the savings went. Anthropic doesn't issue a "we are reducing our prices" press release every quarter — they let the pricing page speak. COREcare does the same with the January disclosure.

#### Lesson 6 — The pricing page itself is a brand surface (Linear)

Linear's pricing page is famously short, transparent, and aesthetically aligned with the product. The pricing page is the first product experience a developer has with Linear. Compare to AxisCare's "request a demo" form that returns a quote three days later. **Implication:** the COREcare pricing page is a P0 design artifact. It needs to load fast, render on mobile (the agency owner Hazel will look at it from her phone in §4 of MRD), and communicate the recommended tier in under 30 seconds. The pricing page IS the demo for the bottom of the SMB funnel.

#### Lesson 7 — Module unbundling is a slow-acting trust poison (Datadog, AlayaCare, Jobber)

Datadog's per-feature add-on stack (APM, RUM, logs, profiling, security monitoring, etc.) has generated extended public complaints about "surprise invoices" — most famously the Coinbase $65M Datadog bill story `[C]`. Jobber's add-on Marketing Suite at $79/mo and AI Receptionist at $99/mo `[C]` are starting to stack the same way — a customer's $349 Grow tier becomes a $527 all-in invoice. AlayaCare's module structure is the same pattern. **Implication:** COREcare bundles aggressively at every tier. We will lose some Year-1 revenue we could have captured by upcharging for advanced reporting or API access. We gain a defensible "no surprise invoices, ever" pricing-page line that compounds over multi-year customer relationships.

#### Lesson 8 — Free tier as funnel works in dev tools, less in vertical SaaS (Notion, Linear, Vercel)

Free tiers function in dev tools because the unit of trial is one developer with no procurement involvement. In private-duty home care, the buyer is the agency owner; there is no equivalent of "I'll just spin up a Hobby account and see." A free tier in COREcare's lane risks adverse selection (1-caregiver hobbyists who never convert) and support cost without revenue. **Implication:** no free tier. Instead, a 14-day free trial with full Growth-tier capability and self-serve onboarding. This is the closest analog to the dev-tools funnel that fits the SMB private-duty motion.

#### Lesson 9 — Usage-based overage is acceptable when the meter is honest (Vercel, AWS)

Vercel and AWS bill on usage. Customers complain when usage spikes are unexpected, but accept the model when the meter is published and projectable. **Implication:** COREcare's per-active-client overage is acceptable — and even celebrated as fair — because (a) it's published, (b) the customer can predict it (active clients change slowly), and (c) it's per-active rather than per-something-volatile-like-API-calls. The recommendation includes a Y1 commitment to surface a "next month's projected bill" view in the admin dashboard, eliminating overage-shock entirely.

#### Lesson 10 — AI as paid add-on is the trap the category is racing into (Notion AI, GitHub Copilot, Toast AI, Jobber AI Receptionist)

Across SaaS, AI features are arriving as paid add-ons priced $10–$20/user/mo. Notion AI at $10/user/mo `[C]`, Jobber AI Receptionist at $99/mo `[C]`, Toast AI tooling priced custom `[unverified]`. The pattern is that vendors capture short-term ARPU lift but customers come to perceive AI as a paid subscription within their subscription. **Implication:** COREcare ships every AI feature included at every tier. This is not generosity — it is positioning. By the time AxisCare Intelligence becomes a $50/mo add-on (likely), COREcare's pricing page can say "AI included since launch, never charged extra." That's a multi-year compounding trust signal.

### 3.3. Pattern Synthesis (Broad Market)

The broad-market lessons converge on a single architectural recommendation: **price for trust, not for short-term ARPA optimization.** Every successful vertical SaaS pricing posture in the comparison set that has held up over 5+ year customer relationships shares the same pattern:

- Published prices
- Bundle hard
- Don't raise prices on existing customers (or do it visibly with notice)
- Make the meter honest and projectable
- Resist the temptation to tier-gate every new feature

Every failure pattern shares the inverse: opaque quoting, à la carte stacking, silent escalators, opaque metering, AI-as-add-on. COREcare's positioning consciously copies the success patterns and refuses the failure patterns.

1. **Per-seat pricing on a high-turnover workforce** — the most attackable surface.
2. **Silent annual escalators** — the slow-acting trust poison.
3. **Custom-quote opacity** — the SMB evaluation friction.
4. **Module unbundling** — the "headline price isn't real price" problem.
5. **Cancellation/data-export friction** — the captive-customer pattern.
6. **Implementation cost shock** — the second-sale opacity.

Every COREcare pricing decision in §7 is reverse-engineered from one or more of these failure modes. The pricing page is, in effect, a published list of refusals to participate in any of them.

## 6. Alternatives Considered & Discarded

This section enumerates ten alternative pricing models considered for COREcare v2 and explains why each was rejected. The objective is to demonstrate that the recommended model is the result of comparative analysis, not first-thought adoption.

### 6.1. Per-Caregiver-Seat Pricing (e.g., ShiftCare model)

**Model:** Charge per active caregiver seat, monthly.

**Why considered:** It's the most common pricing model in adjacent dev tools and project management; customers have learned to read it. MRD §6.3 floats it as a Pro tier option.

**Why rejected:** Directly arms incumbents with the strongest counterargument they have. ShiftCare's documented complaint corpus (§4.1) is the cautionary tale. On a 79% turnover workforce, per-seat is structurally hostile — every caregiver hire is a new bill, every churn is wasted spend. Adopting this model would forfeit the single sharpest competitive weapon COREcare has.

### 6.2. Per-Office-User-Only Pricing (e.g., Generations model)

**Model:** Charge per simultaneous or named office user. Caregivers free.

**Why considered:** It avoids the per-caregiver-seat trap and aligns cost with the buyer (the agency owner cares about office user count, not caregiver count). It's also low-volume (few office users per agency = predictable revenue per customer).

**Why rejected:** Caps revenue ceiling. A 200-active-client Scale-tier agency with 12 office users and per-user-only pricing tops out around $400–$600/mo at any defensible per-user price. This is below COREcare's needed ARPA floor and below incumbent pricing for that customer size — leaves money on the table the customer expects to spend. Also penalizes office-team scale (the after-hours scheduler can't log in) — exact pattern complained of in §4.5.C.

### 6.3. Revenue-Share Pricing (e.g., Careswitch pre-Paradigm model)

**Model:** Charge a percentage of agency revenue (e.g., 1% of monthly billings).

**Why considered:** Customer-friendly framing ("when you grow, we grow"). Aligns vendor incentives with customer success. Was Careswitch's pre-acquisition position [C].

**Why rejected:** Three structural problems:
1. **Variable monthly cost** is operationally hostile to agency owners who run thin margins and need predictable software costs (per [01] ICP analysis).
2. **Collection complexity** — verifying agency revenue requires either trust (vendor takes the customer's word) or integration (expensive to build, invasive to install).
3. **Revenue attribution arguments** — every renewal is a debate about which revenue lines count. Documented friction pattern in revenue-share businesses across categories.

The model is intellectually interesting; it is operationally a nightmare for a one-person company in Year 1.

### 6.4. Visit-Based / Hour-Based Pricing

**Model:** Charge per care visit logged or per care hour delivered (e.g., $0.10 per visit, $0.05 per care hour).

**Why considered:** Volume-based; scales with operational reality; familiar to agency owners who already meter their business in visits and hours.

**Why rejected:** Two problems:
1. **Bill volatility** — high-utilization months produce surprise invoices. Same trust failure as Vercel/AWS overage shock in dev tools.
2. **Perverse incentive on usage** — the customer is incentivized to under-document (skip non-billable visit notes to suppress meter). Damages the documentation depth that is COREcare's product DNA.

The per-active-client unit avoids both — active clients change slowly and predictably, and there is no incentive to under-utilize the platform.

### 6.5. Free-Plus-Paid Tier Model (e.g., Notion / Linear)

**Model:** Free tier with limited functionality; paid tiers for serious use.

**Why considered:** Reduces evaluation friction; bottom-up adoption funnel; classic dev-tools/PLG playbook.

**Why rejected:** Per §3 Lesson 8. The unit of trial in dev tools is one developer; the unit of trial in a private-duty agency is the entire agency (you can't really "try" half a scheduling system). Free tier in this lane invites adverse selection — solo home care contractors who never convert and who consume disproportionate support resource.

**Substitution:** 14-day free trial of full Growth tier, no credit card required, self-serve onboarding. Captures the funnel benefit without the adverse-selection cost.

### 6.6. ServiceTitan-Style Per-Seat-Plus-Implementation

**Model:** Premium per-seat pricing ($245+/seat/mo) plus 5-figure implementation fees plus multi-year contracts.

**Why considered:** It's the Tier-1 vertical SaaS comparison set; the unit economics produce >$30K ARPA per customer; ServiceTitan IPO'd [C] on this model.

**Why rejected:** ICP mismatch. ServiceTitan customers are 50–500-tech HVAC/plumbing operations with 20%+ EBITDA margins and operational sophistication to absorb 6-figure implementation. Private-duty home care SMBs at 15–80 ADC have 5–10% margins and cannot afford the second sale. Adopting this model would forfeit 90%+ of the addressable market in our lane.

### 6.7. AlayaCare-Style Module Unbundling

**Model:** Low base price; charge for everything else (advanced reporting, API access, telehealth, family portal, AI, additional locations) as separately-priced modules.

**Why considered:** Maximizes ARPA expansion within a customer; lets each customer build the price that matches their need; classic enterprise SaaS pattern.

**Why rejected:** §4.4 complaint pattern. The multi-year customer perception is "they nickel and dime us." The headline price stops being the real price; the pricing page becomes meaningless; comparison-shopping breaks down. Every per-feature module gate is a 1-week design conversation we don't want to have, and each module SKU is a billing surface that can break.

### 6.8. Hybrid Per-Active-Client + Per-Visit Overage

**Model:** Base subscription per-active-client; visit-volume overage above tier limits.

**Why considered:** Captures the high-utilization customer where per-active-client alone leaves money on the table.

**Why rejected:** Compound metering complexity. Two meters = two surprise-invoice surfaces = doubled trust risk. Customer mental model becomes harder to explain on the pricing page. The single-meter per-active-client unit captures 90%+ of the economic intent with 50% of the cognitive load.

### 6.9. Marketplace / Take-Rate Model

**Model:** COREcare is free to agencies; revenue comes from a take-rate on marketplace transactions (caregiver staffing fills, EWA, payroll processing, etc.).

**Why considered:** Aligns with the 2026 vertical SaaS playbook of "fintech-on-vertical-SaaS." Toast's processing-fee story; Shopify's payments take. Could produce eventual ACV well above subscription pricing.

**Why rejected:** Multi-year build before revenue. Requires marketplace liquidity (caregiver supply, EWA partner integrations, payment processor relationships) we will not have at v1. Year-1 revenue from this model is ~$0. Subscription revenue is the path to investor-credible ARR by Series A.

**Future option:** Layer take-rate on top of subscription in Year 3+, once distribution and customer trust have been earned. Worth flagging as a strategic option in [03] and [04] Series-A-level conversations.

### 6.10. Tiered-by-ADC-Band (incumbent pattern)

**Model:** Tier customers by ADC band (1–15, 16–50, 51–150, etc.) with custom-quote within each band.

**Why considered:** It's the AxisCare model. Customers are familiar with it. Inside-sales motion is well-understood.

**Why rejected:** Custom-quote opacity is the friction COREcare's published-pricing posture is designed to monetize. Adopting the incumbent tiering model would forfeit the differentiation. Tiering by active-client thresholds (which is what the recommendation does) is the same insight without the custom-quote tax.

### 6.11. Synthesis (Alternatives Considered)

The recommended model — flat tier base + per-active-client overage + unlimited seats + AI included — was selected because it is the **single model that simultaneously**:
- Avoids per-seat pricing on a high-turnover workforce (refutes §4.1)
- Holds prices stable for existing customers (refutes §4.2)
- Publishes the price (refutes §4.3)
- Bundles aggressively (refutes §4.4)
- Serves SMB at the entry point (refutes §4.5)
- Does not charge implementation fees (refutes §4.6)

Every alternative model fails at least one of these tests.

### 7.2. The Trust Architecture (published on pricing page)

**The COREcare Pricing Promise:**

1. **Your price never goes up.** When you sign up, your tier price is locked. Forever. We may change list pricing for new customers; we will not change yours.
2. **Every January, we publish how we did.** Once a year, we publish a one-page disclosure: what our infrastructure cost us per customer, how it changed, and what we did with the savings — features, price reductions, or margin. You see the math.
3. **Cancel any time. One click. No retention call.** Self-serve cancellation in your admin dashboard. Final invoice issued same day. Your data exports to CSV/JSON in one click for 90 days after cancellation.
4. **No surprise invoices.** Your overage projection is in your dashboard, updated daily. We send a notice the moment you cross a tier threshold; the bill is never a surprise.
5. **Implementation is free.** Self-serve onboarding for every tier. Optional white-glove migration is published, capped, fixed-fee.

### 7.4. Sales-Conversation Anchors

For the inbound conversations the published pricing will still trigger — usually from larger agencies trying to validate enterprise fit — the sales motion should anchor on:

1. **The active-client unit.** "You pay for the work you're billing for. Not for staff that turned over six months ago."
2. **Total cost-to-deploy comparison.** Build a one-page TCO calculator that compares COREcare's all-in cost vs. an AlayaCare-class quote (base + modules + implementation + SMS upcharge + AI add-on). The comparison is a 50–65% savings story at the Growth/Scale band.
3. **The trust pledges.** Lead with never-raise. It is the most asymmetric thing on the page and the single highest-impact customer-trust signal in the conversation.

### 7.5. What Is NOT in the Recommendation

To be explicit about boundaries:

- **No free tier.** Trial only (14 days, full Growth capability).
- **No revenue-share.** Subscription only.
- **No per-visit metering.** Active-client only.
- **No multi-year required.** Annual is opt-in for the 10% discount only.
- **No setup fees.** $0 always for self-serve.
- **No module upcharges.** Every feature in a tier is included in that tier's price.
- **No AI-as-add-on.** Included.
- **No SMS-per-message bleed at low volumes.** Included generously; metered only above threshold.
- **No data-export fees, ever.** One-click CSV/JSON, included.
- **No cancellation fees.** Month-to-month customers cancel free; annual customers receive prorated refund of unused months minus the 10% discount.

### 7.6. Pricing Versioning Discipline

Because the never-raise pledge applies to existing customers but allows list-price evolution, the pricing model needs internal versioning discipline:

- **Pricing version is stamped at signup.** Customer record carries `pricing_version_id`. Forever.
- **Pricing changes are released as new versions, not edits.** v1.0 (May 2026 launch) → v1.1 (potential adjustment Q4 2026) → etc.
- **Customer migrations between versions are voluntary.** A customer can opt to migrate to a new version if it benefits them (e.g., we add a feature only available on v1.2). They cannot be force-migrated.
- **Annual disclosure must reference all active versions.** "We have customers on v1.0, v1.1, and v1.2. Each version's customers are honored at their original tier prices."

This is operational discipline, not marketing copy. It needs to be in the data model from launch.

## 9. Defensibility Analysis

This section pressure-tests the recommended pricing against the five most likely competitive responses from incumbents.

### 9.1. Response 1 — AxisCare matches the published-pricing posture

**Probability:** Medium-high. AxisCare with Frontier Growth equity has the operational fuel to publish pricing.

**Their move:** AxisCare publishes "starting at $249/mo" entry pricing on a comparable feature set. Removes their custom-quote friction.

**COREcare counter:** Three layers of defense:
1. **The never-raise pledge** — AxisCare cannot match this without exposing their existing-customer pricing history. Their PE ownership pattern is incompatible with no-price-increase commitments.
2. **The annual disclosure** — same structural conflict. AxisCare cannot publish per-customer cost data without exposing margin to competitors and to PE-driven price-pressure conversations.
3. **The active-client unit** — AxisCare's existing customer base is on per-deal-quoted contracts that won't cleanly migrate to a published per-active-client model. Migration friction protects COREcare's wedge for 12–24 months.

**Verdict:** Defensible. AxisCare can match price; cannot match trust architecture without structural change incompatible with their PE backing.

### 9.2. Response 2 — AlayaCare bundles modules into a flat published price

**Probability:** Low-medium. AlayaCare has signaled enterprise focus (CIBC facility Feb 2026 [C]) and SMB simplification is off-strategy.

**Their move:** AlayaCare launches an "AlayaCare Essentials" SMB SKU at flat $899/mo with a defined feature subset.

**COREcare counter:** The "Essentials" pattern is well-documented as a downmarket trap — the mothership's enterprise customers feel the SMB SKU undermines their pricing; the SMB SKU's customers feel they're getting a stripped product. Toast tried this with "Toast Now" during COVID and quietly retired it.

**Verdict:** Highly defensible. AlayaCare's strategic posture makes a clean SMB pivot unlikely.

### 9.3. Response 3 — Aaniie undercuts on per-active-client price

**Probability:** Medium. Aaniie's pricing is already published and competitive ($13/active client Pro tier).

**Their move:** Aaniie drops Pro tier per-active-client price to $8 to match COREcare Scale tier overage.

**COREcare counter:** Aaniie's challenge is **AI inclusion at the entry SKU**. The $195 entry tier doesn't include the AI features that are now table-stakes [C — per Aaniie pricing page]. COREcare bundles AI at Starter ($349). The price comparison is Aaniie at $195 + AI add-on (whatever they charge) vs. COREcare $349 all-in. As soon as Aaniie's AI add-on is north of $100/mo, the comparison flips in COREcare's favor.

**Verdict:** Defensible on AI bundling. Vulnerable on raw per-active-client price; the differentiation must lean on AI inclusion + caregiver-mobile-first UX from [02].

### 9.4. Response 4 — A new entrant copies the trust architecture

**Probability:** Medium over 24 months. The architecture is observable; the defensibility is the cumulative track record.

**Their move:** A new vertical SaaS startup launches with identical never-raise + annual disclosure positioning.

**COREcare counter:** Cumulative disclosure record is the moat. By year 3, COREcare has three published disclosures showing actual cost-savings allocation to features and customer benefit. A new entrant in year 3 has zero. Customers will discount the trust signal of a new entrant who hasn't yet had to keep the promise across a difficult business cycle.

**Verdict:** First-mover advantage on trust architecture is durable but not absolute. The window to lock in the brand association is 12–18 months from launch.

### 9.5. Response 5 — Incumbents collude on per-seat-floor messaging

**Probability:** Low. Incumbents do not coordinate; the per-seat model is structurally entrenched but not a coordinated position.

**Their move:** Industry consultants and analyst groups (Home Care Pulse, etc.) push the narrative that per-active-client pricing creates "billing surprises" relative to per-seat predictability.

**COREcare counter:** The COREcare admin dashboard's projected-bill view (§7.2 #4) inverts the narrative: COREcare's bill is **more** predictable than per-seat (active-client counts change slowly; staff hires/churn churn rapidly). The data supports the inversion.

**Verdict:** Defensible. The narrative attack is reversible with product evidence.

## 10. Risks & Mitigation

### 10.1. Risk: Mix-weighted ARPA underperforms MRD §6.3 target

**Risk:** Y1 mix-weighted ARPA lands at $8K–$9K vs. MRD §6.3 $15K–$25K target. Investor narrative on ARPA expansion underperforms.

**Mitigation:**
- Update MRD §6.3 ARPA range to $10K–$18K Y1 → $15K–$22K Y3 in next MRD revision.
- Frame for [03] investor conversations: lower ARPA at higher logo volume is intentional vertical-SaaS oxygen-squeeze playbook (Toast, Jobber comparison set).
- Y3 ARPA expansion path: Scale-tier mix shift + overage adoption + (optionally) Y3+ fintech layer per §6.9.

### 10.2. Risk: Never-raise pledge limits pricing power

**Risk:** Existing-customer pricing locks reduce ARR expansion levers.

**Mitigation:**
- Pledge applies to existing customers only — list pricing for new customers can move with brand strength and product depth.
- Modeled in §8.3 sensitivity: even with no existing-customer increases, 115%+ NRR via mix shift and overage is achievable.
- The trust signal value (lower CAC, lower churn) outweighs the foregone pricing-power optionality at this stage. Reassess at Series B.

### 10.3. Risk: Annual disclosure becomes a competitive intelligence gift

**Risk:** Publishing per-customer cost data lets competitors model COREcare's gross margin and target price competition.

**Mitigation:**
- Disclosure is aggregated and ranged (not per-customer-itemized). "Average per-customer infra cost: $X.XX/mo, range $Y–$Z."
- The information competitors gain is modest (any competent analyst can model it from public cloud pricing).
- The trust-signal value to customers vastly exceeds the competitive risk.

### 10.4. Risk: AI cost increases from Anthropic / provider compress margin

**Risk:** Anthropic raises model prices; COREcare cannot pass through; margin compresses.

**Mitigation:**
- Provider-neutral abstraction layer (per MRD §7 dependency note) allows model substitution.
- Caching aggressive (already modeled at 90% cache discount in §5).
- Cost-model headroom: 76–80% gross margin at recommended pricing tolerates 2–3x increase in AI cost line item before margin drops below 65%.
- The asymmetric trust architecture (vs. hard pass-through) preserves the optionality to absorb an increase.

### 10.5. Risk: Self-serve onboarding fails for non-trivial agency setups

**Risk:** Free implementation on Starter/Growth means onboarding cost is buried in support; if onboarding takes >5 hours of human time per customer, unit economics collapse.

**Mitigation:**
- AI-assisted onboarding (the new customer's setup is itself a Sonnet/Haiku conversation) caps human time.
- White-glove migration is an upsell ($1,500 fixed-fee on Growth, included on Scale) for customers who genuinely cannot self-serve.
- Track time-to-go-live as a P0 metric; if median >7 days, reassess.

### 10.6. Risk: Stripe / payment processor relationship interruption

**Risk:** Subscription billing dependency on Stripe; account suspension or rate change disrupts revenue collection.

**Mitigation:**
- Standard commercial risk; Stripe is dominant for SaaS subscription billing.
- Backup processor (e.g., Adyen) onboarded by Y2.
- Direct ACH fallback for Scale-tier customers.

### 10.7. Risk: A Tier-1 incumbent acquires a published-pricing competitor and adopts the model

**Risk:** Per [04], the M&A landscape is active. AlayaCare-class acquirer buys Aaniie or similar and inherits a published-pricing playbook.

**Mitigation:**
- Trust architecture is a process commitment, not a feature. Acquirer cultures struggle to adopt customer-trust pledges (PE ownership patterns are structurally incompatible).
- Acquisition friction (rebrand period, integration tax) creates 12–24 months of relative quiet during which COREcare can entrench.
- COREcare itself becomes a more attractive acquisition target if a competitor can't replicate the architecture organically — the strategic acquirer math from [04] is reinforced, not undermined.
