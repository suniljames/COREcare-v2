# Home Health Tech Market: Q1 2026 Shakes Up the Landscape

**The US home healthcare technology vendor market entered 2026 with a burst of M&A activity, an AI product arms race among incumbents, and a landmark regulatory win for hospital-at-home.** Three developments stand out above all others: the $1.1 billion Kinderhook/Enhabit take-private signals renewed PE conviction in home health; the five-year extension of the CMS hospital-at-home waiver through 2030 removes the single biggest overhang on care-at-home investment; and enterprise-scale AI documentation deployments—notably Compassus rolling ambient AI to 8,000+ clinicians across 33 states—mark the moment AI crossed from pilot to production in home-based care. Meanwhile, every major EHR vendor launched AI-native features, Netsmart may be heading for a sale, and CMS finalized a far milder reimbursement cut than feared—collectively creating the most favorable environment for home health technology investment since 2021.

---

## M&A: PE-backed consolidation roars back to life

The January–April 2026 window produced more significant home health M&A than any comparable period in recent years, driven overwhelmingly by private equity.

**The Kinderhook Industries acquisition of Enhabit (NYSE: EHAB) for $1.1 billion**, announced February 24, is the headline deal. Kinderhook is taking Enhabit private at $13.80 per share—a 24% premium—acquiring 249 home health and 117 hospice locations across 34 states. CEO Barb Jacobsmeyer plans to depart in July 2026. Under private ownership, Enhabit will pivot toward value-based care and AI-enabled operations, backed by a $315 million term loan and $160 million revolving facility. Kinderhook's 20-year track record in home health suggests this is a bet on operational transformation, not financial engineering.

**General Atlantic's $3 billion acquisition of TEAM Services Group** from Alpine Investors, completed in early April, is the largest home-based care PE deal of 2026. TEAM operates tech-enabled payroll, HR, and financial management infrastructure for consumer-directed care across all 50 states with 100,000 caregivers—positioning it as critical back-office plumbing for the industry.

In the technology vendor space specifically, **LivTech (backed by PSG) acquired Alora Healthcare Systems on April 9**, creating a unified post-acute and home-based care platform combining LivTech's operational tools with Alora's AI-enhanced clinical and compliance software. This is the only direct home health software vendor acquisition in the period. Other notable deals include Ares Management and DaVita jointly investing in Elara Caring (February 2) to co-develop a kidney-specific home care model, Aveanna Healthcare acquiring Family First Homecare from Trivest Partners for pediatric home care expansion, and DispatchHealth divesting its imaging business to TridentCare on January 21 to sharpen its hospital-at-home focus post-Medically Home merger.

**None of the major named technology vendors changed hands.** WellSky (TPG/Leonard Green), PointClickCare (Hellman & Friedman/Dragoneer), MatrixCare (ResMed), HCHB (Hearst Health), Axxess, and HHAeXchange (Hg) all remain with their current owners. However, **Netsmart may be approaching the market**: PE Hub reported that co-owners GI Partners and TA Associates planned to launch a Netsmart sale process in early 2026, their second attempt to exit the approximately $250 million EBITDA company in two years. This would be the most consequential home health tech vendor transaction if it proceeds.

On the payer side, **no new vertical integration moves** into home health technology by CVS Health, UnitedHealth/Optum, or Humana were identified. These companies completed their major home health acquisitions in 2022–2023 and appear to be in integration mode. No IPO filings or SPAC activity materialized for any home health tech vendor.

## Funding: capital concentrates at the top while unicorns go quiet

The broader digital health funding environment improved meaningfully in Q1 2026—**$4 billion across 110 deals**, up from $3 billion across 122 deals in Q1 2025, with average deal size of **$36.7 million**, the highest since Q4 2021. But 59% of funding concentrated in just 12 mega-rounds of $100 million or more, and Series B raises are declining, signaling persistent challenges for early-stage companies trying to scale.

Within home health technology specifically, funding activity was modest:

- **AlayaCare secured a $50 million growth capital facility** from CIBC Innovation Banking (February 20), earmarked for continued growth and strategic M&A. This is debt-like growth capital rather than equity, following AlayaCare's $225 million CAD Series D in 2021.
- **Sage raised a $65 million Series C** led by Goldman Sachs Alternatives (March 5) for its AI-powered senior living and skilled nursing platform—adjacent to but not directly in home health.
- **Health Recovery Solutions acquired Rimidi** (March 3), adding chronic disease management and CGM integration capabilities to its RPM platform, with Rimidi founder Dr. Lucienne Ide becoming HRS's Chief Medical Officer.
- **Baba emerged from stealth with $6.5 million in seed funding** for a Medicare/MA patient advocacy platform partnering with home care organizations.

The three home health unicorns from the 2021 vintage—**Honor ($1.25B), Papa ($1.4B), and DispatchHealth ($1.7B)**—have not raised new equity since 2021–2022. No down rounds were publicly reported, but the extended funding drought amid a cautious venture environment suggests current private valuations may not reflect their peak marks. DispatchHealth has been the most operationally active, completing its Medically Home merger and divesting non-core assets, while Papa launched its strategic Papa Plus product in March and Honor continues operating the world's largest home care network through Home Instead.

## Regulatory shifts: a milder cut, a longer runway, and an AI controversy

**CMS delivered a 1.3% net aggregate payment decrease** (~$220 million) for home health in 2026—dramatically softer than the proposed 6.4% ($1.13 billion) cut. The final 30-day episode payment rate is **$2,038.22**, reflecting a 2.4% market basket update offset by a permanent -1.023% PDGM behavioral adjustment and a -3.0% temporary recoupment. Industry lobbying successfully reduced both the permanent adjustment (from -4.059%) and the temporary adjustment (from -5.0%). This marks the fourth consecutive year of permanent cuts, but the magnitude is far below what was feared.

**The five-year extension of the Acute Hospital Care at Home (AHCAH) waiver through September 30, 2030** is arguably the most consequential regulatory development. The Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2026, signed in February, ended months of uncertainty after the waiver briefly lapsed during a government shutdown in late 2025. Nearly **400 approved programs** across 139–147 health systems in 37–39 states now have a stable five-year planning horizon. The law also requires HHS to evaluate home-based versus traditional inpatient care outcomes and extended Medicare telehealth flexibilities for two years.

**OASIS-E2 went live on April 1, 2026**, with several notable changes: the COVID-19 vaccination item (O0350) was retired, gender reporting shifted from the M0069 framework to A0810 ("Sex"—male/female only), falls reporting was tightened to require coding intercepted falls with major injury, and four SDOH assessment items were removed before collection began. Most consequentially for technology vendors, **the draft OASIS-E2 guidance manual included language restricting clinicians from using automated systems to generate or populate OASIS responses directly**—drawing significant industry pushback given the rapid deployment of AI documentation tools. This tension between AI adoption and regulatory guardrails is now the most closely watched compliance issue in home health tech.

Other regulatory developments include Missouri activating EVV hard edits on April 1 (claims without matching EVV data are now denied), TEFCA reaching **500 million health records exchanged** across 14,214 organizations, and ONC publishing the HTI-5 proposed rule proposing to eliminate over 50% of health IT certification criteria while refocusing on FHIR-based APIs and AI-enabled interoperability—estimated to save $1.53 billion and 1.4 million compliance hours. Seven states introduced bills targeting AI downcoding of claims without physician oversight, and the HHVBP model added four new quality measures for 2026 including Medicare Spending Per Beneficiary Post-Acute Care.

## The AI arms race: every major vendor now shipping AI features

The January–April 2026 period marks the definitive transition of AI from competitive differentiator to table stakes across home health technology. Every major vendor either launched or expanded AI capabilities, with ambient documentation and intelligent workflow automation as the dominant use cases.

**WellSky executed the most aggressive AI product cadence**, launching five AI-powered products in four months: ambient listening technology promising 50% documentation time reduction (January 12), AI-powered referral intake (January 27), WellSky Summarize for personal care (February 5), SkySense AI expansion to long-term care (February 11), and CarePort Referral Intake with AI integration (March 2). On April 14, WellSky announced an expanded Google Cloud partnership embedding advanced AI into CarePort's provider directory.

**PointClickCare matched WellSky's intensity** with its own AI suite: Referral Advisor launched at the JP Morgan Healthcare Conference (January 13), Discharge Intel debuted at HIMSS26 (March 9–12) showing a 32% reduction in SNF-related readmissions at the University of Maryland, a next-generation EHR for Practice Groups with embedded Ambient Scribe launched March 30, and Study Buddy—the first AI-powered research platform for real-world evidence from LTC data—launched April 9. New CPTO David Pessis, appointed in September 2025, is driving what PointClickCare calls an "AI-first vision."

**HCHB launched Curate: Scribe on March 12**, an AI-assisted documentation tool developed with StenoHealth and embedded natively in the HCHB EHR workflow. HCHB's broader Curate platform strategy includes additional AI-enabled solutions planned for H1 2026. **AlayaCare unveiled AlayaFlow** on March 3, featuring autonomous AI agents that independently handle complex workflows end-to-end, including scheduling and documentation, with claims of helping agencies "reclaim 80% of time and costs." **MatrixCare partnered with nVoq** (March 24) to bring AI-powered dictation and ambient conversation summarization to its EHR. **Netsmart showcased an Amazon Connect Health-powered agentic AI** at HIMSS26 that generates ICD-10, CPT, and HCPCS codes from clinical notes.

Outside the traditional vendor landscape, the most significant deployment was **Compassus partnering with Nestmed** (January 26) for what is described as the largest AI deployment in home-based care—ambient AI documentation rolled out to **8,000+ clinicians across 33 states**. VNS Health followed on April 1 as the first New York organization to scale ambient AI documentation enterprise-wide. A JAMA study published in early 2026 showed AI ambient scribes decreased total EHR time by 13.4 minutes per encounter across five academic medical centers.

## Emerging vendors: consolidation, pivots, and legal troubles

Several emerging and specialized vendors saw material changes in this period. **IntelyCare's acquisition of CareRev on January 8** created the first full-spectrum healthcare labor platform spanning both acute and post-acute staffing. Both brands continue operating under IntelyCare CEO Matthew Levesque, combining CareRev's 35,000+ healthcare professionals with IntelyCare's home health and post-acute network. IntelyCare achieved a $1.1 billion valuation after its 2022 Series C.

**Papa launched Papa Plus on March 4**, a strategic evolution integrating companion care with targeted quality programs for health plans and guaranteeing ROI for CMS Star Ratings, utilization, and retention outcomes. The company also hired a new CTO (Thomas Carlough from Wider Circle) and SVP of Healthcare Strategy, signaling a technology-forward pivot. **DispatchHealth launched a new hospital-at-home joint venture with Saint Francis Health System** in Tulsa (February 6) while continuing to streamline post-merger with Medically Home.

**Biofourmis faces reputational headwinds** after former CEO and co-founder Kuldeep Singh Rajput was charged in Singapore on February 4 with seven criminal counts including falsification of accounts involving $16.5 million in allegedly fake invoices. The company—now merged with CopilotIQ under CEO David Koretz—continues operating its AI-driven in-home care platform with approximately 144 employees.

**ShiftKey appointed Mandy Long as board chair** (January 27), bringing AI analytics expertise from BigBear.ai and IBM Watson Health. **AxisCare earned its first Best in KLAS award** in Personal Care while winning a record 19 G2 badges and hosting its inaugural EVOLVE user conference. **TytoCare's Teladoc Health integration** began its phased rollout in 2026, embedding FDA-cleared remote physical exam capabilities into Teladoc's 24/7 Care and Primary360 programs.

**Careswitch** emerged as a notable new entrant with its "Looper" AI agent that automates dispatch, billing, and route optimization, targeting the $10–100 million agency segment—an underserved middle market.

## Market dynamics: $4.7B market, 80% turnover, and the AI adoption gap

Market sizing estimates for home health software in 2026 range from **$4.5–4.7 billion** (narrow scope, per Future Market Insights and Mordor Intelligence) to **$14.4 billion** (broader scope including telehealth and RPM software, per Research and Markets), with CAGR projections of **8–16%** depending on definition. The global home-based care market is projected to exceed $300 billion. Cloud-based deployment now accounts for 47–58% of the market by share and is growing at 14.5% annually.

The workforce crisis shows no signs of abating. **Caregiver turnover approaches 80% annually**, with 70% quitting within their first 100 days. The BLS projects **740,000 additional home care workers needed** over the next decade against a supply gap where only 5.1 million workers are available for 8.9 million projected openings. Home health providers report turning away more than 25% of referred patients due to staffing shortages. HCHB's State of Home-Based Care 2026 report found referral conversions dropped 13% since 2018, with **4.2 million individuals not receiving recommended home health care in 2024**.

The AI adoption gap remains stark despite vendor activity: **fewer than 25% of home care organizations have made AI-specific investments**, even as 60% of leaders believe AI will have the greatest operational impact by 2030. This disconnect between vendor supply and buyer adoption represents both a risk and an opportunity—the vendors who simplify AI deployment within existing workflows (as HCHB's Curate: Scribe and AlayaCare's AlayaFlow attempt) will likely capture disproportionate share.

## What matters most for market intelligence

Five developments from this period should materially update any existing market analysis:

**The hospital-at-home waiver extension through 2030** transforms the investment thesis for companies like DispatchHealth, Current Health, and health systems building home hospital programs. A five-year runway enables capital commitments that a year-to-year extension never could. Expect accelerated program launches and technology procurement cycles.

**The Netsmart potential sale** could produce the largest pure-play home health tech vendor transaction in years. At roughly $250 million EBITDA, a sale at typical healthcare IT multiples (15–20x) would price Netsmart at $3.75–5 billion, setting a new benchmark for the sector and potentially triggering competitive responses from WellSky and PointClickCare's PE backers.

**AI documentation at enterprise scale** has moved from differentiation to expectation. Compassus (8,000 clinicians), VNS Health (enterprise-wide), and the AI product launches from WellSky, PointClickCare, HCHB, AlayaCare, and MatrixCare collectively indicate that by year-end 2026, ambient AI documentation will be a standard feature rather than a premium add-on. The OASIS-E2 guidance language restricting automated OASIS population is the critical regulatory wildcard—if enforced strictly, it could slow AI adoption precisely as vendors are shipping these capabilities.

**PE confidence in home health is rising**, evidenced by the $1.1 billion Enhabit take-private, the $3 billion TEAM Services deal, and accelerating add-on activity across multiple platforms. PitchBook reported a 22.4% increase in home-based care deal flow in 2025, and early 2026 is described as a "strong start." The investment thesis has evolved from pure reimbursement arbitrage to technology-enabled operational transformation and value-based care positioning.

**The 2026 Best in KLAS rankings** reshuffled competitive positioning: MatrixCare won Home Health and Hospice for the seventh consecutive year, HCHB held second in Home Health, WellSky placed third, and AxisCare took the Personal Care category. PointClickCare dominated SNF/LTC (seventh consecutive year) and won Senior Living for the first time. These rankings directly influence procurement decisions and vendor short-listing.
