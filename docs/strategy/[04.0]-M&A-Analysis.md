# The home care SaaS M&A window is open but narrow

**Bottom line:** A US private-duty/non-medical home care SaaS vendor at $5–75M ARR most likely exits via a **PE sponsor-to-sponsor or strategic bolt-on transaction at 5–10x ARR within a 24-month window**, with a realistic exit probability of 50–65% for quality assets and near-zero IPO probability at this scale. The 2026 environment is uniquely favorable: **PE dry powder at record healthcare levels** (Bain: $191B of 2025 healthcare PE deal value, an all-time high), aging sponsor portfolios at every obvious strategic buyer (WellSky, HHAeXchange, Netsmart, PointClickCare), and four forcing regulatory catalysts hitting simultaneously (OASIS‑E2 April 2026, 80/20 Medicaid HCBS rule, EVV enforcement acceleration, and hospital‑at‑home waiver expansion). However, the Netsmart 2024 failed sale and a public software multiple compression back to ~3.6x NTM median (Feb 2026, Clouded Judgement) establish that **scale and narrative alone no longer clear a sponsor‑to‑sponsor process** — Rule of 40, embedded AI, regulatory moat, and clean customer satisfaction data now matter more than ever. Sellers should plan for a **two-stage private exit** (growth equity → PE platform → larger PE/strategic), target HHAeXchange, AlayaCare, WellSky, LivTech, Activated Insights, Axxess, and Paradigm as the most probable 2026 strategic buyers, and treat the current 12–18 months as the optimal window before the 2025 vintage of PE sponsor holds matures and competing processes crowd the market. All dollar values and multiples are flagged below as confirmed [C] or estimated [EST].

## The home care tech deal map from 2018 to April 2026

The last seven years of home care and post-acute software M&A cleanly divide into four sub-waves that established today's competitive structure. **ResMed's $750M acquisition of MatrixCare in November 2018** (25x pro-forma EBITDA on ~$30M [EST]) following its $800M Brightree deal (2016) and HEALTHCAREfirst tuck-in (2018) created the first healthcare-device strategic attempting to become a post-acute software conglomerate. The second wave was **WellSky's private-duty beachhead**: ClearCare in October 2019 (terms undisclosed but ~8 of top 10 US agencies at acquisition, rebranded WellSky Personal Care in 2021), followed by Consolo (2018, hospice), Experience Care (2023, LTC), and Bonafide in October 2024 (DME/HME workflow, sold by DCA Partners). WellSky's own 2020 recapitalization brought Leonard Green in alongside TPG as 50/50 sponsors at a **reported >$3B enterprise value [EST]** — a valuation that now anchors the upper bound of what a consolidated home-based-care software platform trades for.

The **HHAeXchange consolidation** represents the third wave and the single most important consolidation event for any private-duty SaaS to understand. Under Hg Capital and Cressey (investment announced September 2021, terms undisclosed), HHAeXchange executed a Medicaid HCBS/EVV roll-up: **Annkissam (2023), Generations Homecare System (July 2024), Cashé Software (2024), and the landmark Sandata acquisition (October 2024)** from Accel-KKR. Sandata had 23 state Medicaid EVV contracts and 20,000+ agencies; combining the two largest HCBS/EVV platforms under one owner creates a de facto duopoly with WellSky Personal Care in private-duty software. No deal values were disclosed, but the pace (four acquisitions in 24 months) and scope imply HHAeXchange is now a multi-billion-dollar platform actively hunting bolt-ons.

The fourth wave is the **2025–2026 AI and services convergence**. Paradigm acquired Careswitch in September 2025 (undisclosed; Paradigm processes ~30% of VA HCBS spend and positions Careswitch's agentic AI as its operating system layer). LivTech (PSG Equity-backed, led by ex-Francisco Partners CFO Ron Kozlin) acquired Alora Healthcare Systems on **April 9, 2026**, declaring intent to build "the category leader in care-at-home technology." IntelyCare merged with CareRev on **January 8, 2026** (combining post-acute and acute staffing marketplaces; IntelyCare was last valued at $1.1B in 2022). Activated Insights (Cressey-backed) acquired **CareAcademy on November 12, 2025** (800K caregivers trained, 2,000+ providers) after acquiring WeCare Connect in April 2025 — building a workforce-experience layer across 16,000+ LTPAC provider sites. On the services side, **Kinderhook Industries' $1.1B take-private of Enhabit** (announced Feb 23, 2026; $13.80/share, ~24.4% premium, ~10x 2026E earnings [EST]) and **General Atlantic's ~$3B buyout of TEAM Services Group** from Alpine Investors (closed late March 2026, ~10x EBITDA [EST per Bloomberg]) signal renewed large-check PE appetite for home-based care platforms.

One transaction's absence looms over the entire landscape: **Netsmart's failed 2024 sale process**. GI Partners and TA Associates hired Goldman Sachs and William Blair, targeting **>$5B enterprise value (~$250M EBITDA, mid-to-high-teens multiple) [EST]**. First-round bids were collected in March 2024. **No transaction closed.** PE Hub reports GI/TA relaunched a second sale process in early 2026. This failure — the largest attempted healthcare IT sponsor-to-sponsor exit of 2024 — is the single most important cautionary data point for anyone calibrating 2026 valuation expectations.

## Adjacent market deals that reshape the acquirer pool

**Roper Technologies' $1.65B net / $1.85B headline acquisition of CentralReach in March 2025** (sold by Insight Partners; expected contribution of $175M revenue / $75M EBITDA — implying ~**9x revenue, ~22x EBITDA**) is the most relevant adjacent exit benchmark in IDD/HCBS software. Roper explicitly cited "niche market leadership," 200,000+ professionals on platform, and 20%+ organic growth as justifications — a template that a scaled private-duty platform could directly emulate. CentralReach has since executed three bolt-ons (Behavior Science Technology in September 2024, SpectrumAI and AI Measures in August 2025), demonstrating Roper's willingness to fund continued tuck-ins. In the same IDD/HCBS category, **CaseWorthy (STG-backed) acquired a majority of MediSked in January 2023** (undisclosed), consolidating another HCBS care-management platform.

Senior living technology consolidation has produced a parallel roll-up under Vista Equity's iN2L platform: **Vista's growth investment in iN2L (March 2021), followed by iN2L/LifeLoop merger (April 2022), Caremerge engagement business carve-out to VoiceFriend/Icon (June 2022), and LifeLoop/Linked Senior merger (July 2024)** — reaching ~500K residents across 4,700 communities. This pattern (PE platform + serial tucks into a "workforce/resident experience" layer) directly mirrors the Activated Insights thesis and signals that private-duty home care is likely to see the same consolidation in its workforce-adjacent tools.

Caregiver staffing marketplaces show a different dynamic — **ShiftKey's $300M Series A / majority recap in January 2023 at a $2B valuation** (led by Lorient Capital via Ares Secondaries/Pantheon continuation vehicle; Clearlake and Health Velocity Capital participating) stands out as the high-water mark and the only disclosed valuation in the segment. **Honor Technology's August 2021 acquisition of Home Instead** (undisclosed, combined ~$2.1B revenue [EST]) and **IntelyCare/CareRev (January 2026)** are unpriced strategic combinations rather than exits.

## SMB vertical SaaS comparables reveal brutal multiple compression

The ServiceTitan trajectory is the single most important external comparable and the most sobering. ServiceTitan priced its December 12, 2024 IPO at $71 (raised from a $52–57 initial range to $65–67 to final $71), closed its first day at $101 (+42%), and reached a peak market cap of ~$8.9B on implied **~12x TTM revenue**. By April 2026, the stock trades ~$58–63, market cap ~$5.99B, on FY2026 revenue of $961M (+24.5% YoY) — implying **~6.2x TTM revenue, a ~50% multiple compression in sixteen months despite continued 24–27% growth**. ServiceTitan's own acquisition history — particularly **FieldRoutes at $577M in January 2022** (likely ~15–20x ARR at peak-cycle ZIRP pricing [EST]) — is no longer replicable; the same deal in 2026 would likely transact at 30–50% lower multiples.

**Procare Solutions/Roper ($1.86B, January 2024)** is the most relevant strategic-exit benchmark: sold by Warburg Pincus and TA Associates at **~9–12x revenue / ~18–26x EBITDA** on ~$100M projected 2023 EBITDA and ~$150–200M revenue [EST]. Like CentralReach, Procare had a proven multi-year PE roll-up history (Cirrus Group, KidReports, Kinderlime, SchoolLeader tuck-ins under Warburg/TA), and Roper paid for **category leadership plus clean EBITDA**.

Toast (restaurant SaaS) has emerged from IPO volatility at a market cap of **~$15–16B on $6.15B FY2025 revenue, $342M net income, and $2.05B ARR** — roughly **2.5x TTM revenue but ~7.5x ARR** (the spread reflects payment pass-through). Procore (construction) trades at ~6–7x revenue, down from >20x at 2021 IPO. Jobber's **General Atlantic–led $100M Series D in February 2023** (previously stating 3x revenue growth since January 2021; total funding $191M) implies a **~$1.2–1.8B valuation [EST]**, though both Jobber and Housecall Pro have deliberately avoided disclosing public marks since 2022 — a strong insider signal that they view 2021 valuations as uninvestable. **BuildOps' March 2025 $1B Series C** (led by Meritech on undisclosed revenue) is a venture price rather than an exit comp.

The clear implication for a home care SaaS: aspire to the **Procare/Roper model (category leader sold at 9–12x revenue with proven EBITDA)**, not the ServiceTitan/FieldRoutes era (15–20x ARR on growth alone). Fintech attach (payroll processing, Medicaid claims RCM) boosts ARR multiple even when it depresses headline revenue multiple.

## Current valuation multiples by scenario

The **February 2026 "agentic AI" software selloff** (sometimes called the "Anthropic Effect" or "Claude Cowork Event") reset the backdrop. Public SaaS multiples fell ~20–30% from early-2025 levels: the **SaaS Capital Index dropped from 7.0x to 4.8x run-rate revenue** (Jan 2026), **Clouded Judgement median NTM multiple fell to 3.6x** (Feb 2026, historic low outside COVID crash), and the BVP Nasdaq Emerging Cloud Index sits at ~6.3x NTM. Healthcare IT specifically trades at a **10–20% discount to general cloud peers** per Bessemer's State of Health AI 2026, reflecting lingering Health Tech 1.0 trust damage — but the discount inverts for AI-native healthcare SaaS with proprietary data and clinical traction.

**Earnout expectations for $5–75M ARR healthcare IT deals: 20–35% of total consideration over 2–3 years**, tied to revenue/ARR targets and/or EBITDA thresholds. SRS Acquiom's 2025 Deal Terms Study finds that earnouts pay only **~$0.21 per $1 of maximum structured value** across industries, and only 59% of earnouts result in any partial payment — sellers should negotiate caps but assume minimal realization at the margin. Representative recent deal structures include Health Catalyst/Upfront (January 2025, $86M closing + $33.4M earnout = 28% earnout share) and Sanara MedTech/CarePICS (three-year initial plus ten-year value earnout tied to patient volume).

The **AI premium is real but bifurcated**. Rock Health reports ~50% of 2025 digital health deals were AI-enabled, AI companies captured 54% of total digital health funding, and Series C rounds saw a 61% "AI premium." Notable healthcare AI valuations: **Abridge at $5.3B post-money on $117M contracted ARR** (June 2025, ~45x ARR [EST]); **Commure at $6B in June 2025** (~30x ARR [EST]); **Waystar/Iodine Software in July 2025** (~$1.25B on ~$100M ARR = ~12x [EST]). But these are VC/growth rounds, not buyout exits — **true M&A multiples for AI-enabled healthcare SaaS run 30–50% below these marks**. "AI wrappers" (thin LLM layers without proprietary data) are being repriced downward sharply.

## The acquirer set narrows to a dozen realistic names

The research identifies twelve buyers with realistic probability of closing a qualifying private-duty home care SaaS acquisition in 2026. The top tier is dominated by sponsor-backed strategics already running tuck-in programs.

**HHAeXchange (~75% probability [EST])** is the most aggressive serial consolidator in the space — four acquisitions in 24 months under Hg Capital and Cressey, with an explicit private-pay gap versus WellSky Personal Care. **AlayaCare (~65% [EST])** secured a $50M CIBC debt facility in February 2026 explicitly earmarked for M&A, acquired Nightingale Software (October 2025) and Arrow Solutions (2024), and is in aggressive US Medicaid + private-duty expansion mode. **WellSky (~55% [EST])** has the largest installed PD base via ClearCare/Personal Care but faces documented customer satisfaction decline and R&D under-investment; incremental bolt-ons to plug product gaps (AI scribe, family engagement, EVV refresh) are likely. **Activated Insights (~55% [EST])** is the definitive workforce-experience roll-up (CareAcademy, WeCare Connect, Home Care Pulse, Pinnacle) and would pursue workforce-tech assets specifically, not agency management. **LivTech (PSG Equity, ~50% [EST])** just declared category intent through the Alora deal and is hiring for M&A scale.

Second tier acquirers with 25–35% probability each: **Axxess** (founder-owned but just acquired Complia Health 2024/25), **Paradigm** (Careswitch thesis extends to agency management), **Jonas Software/Constellation** (US PD gap after TurnPoint's AU deal; patient on competitive auctions), and **Netsmart** (stretched by its own sale process but capable of PD-adjacent bolt-ons like its SAMMS Healthcare Group deal May 2025). Lower probability names include **PointClickCare** (~20%, SNF/senior living focus), **Kinderhook** (~15% for software specifically, services-led thesis), and **direct Hg Capital** (~12%, would channel through HHAeXchange).

**Payers and Big Tech are notably absent from the realistic list at this ARR scale.** Optum's 2025–2026 focus was Amedisys integration (closed August 2025) and internal tech stack; Humana/CenterWell has never demonstrated software M&A patterns; CVS/Aetna is focused on Signify Health/Oak Street integration. Microsoft, Oracle, Salesforce, and Amazon have demonstrated no appetite for $5–75M ARR private-duty targets. These entities would only emerge as buyers for the eventual consolidated platform at $200M+ ARR scale.

## Exit pathways converge on private-market transitions

**No pure-play home care SaaS has ever IPO'd in the US.** PointClickCare went public on the TSX in 2014 and was taken private by Dragoneer and JMI in 2017; it has now added Hellman & Friedman and has stayed private for nine years across three sponsor cycles despite reaching $500M+ revenue with ~20% growth. The Health Tech 1.0 IPO class of 2019–2021 is a cautionary case: Phreesia, Health Catalyst, Privia, and Definitive Healthcare all trade well below IPO prices five-plus years later, with **Definitive Healthcare trading at ~$1.40–1.57 in April 2026** (95%+ destruction from 2021 peak). The Health Tech 2.0 class (Waystar, Tempus AI, Hinge Health) has performed better, but all priced at **$100M+ ARR with Rule of 40 >60% and a differentiated AI narrative** — Hinge Health specifically priced below its last private round and still posts 98% client retention, 117% NDR, and 72% annualized growth.

**PE sponsor-to-sponsor sales are the dominant exit path.** WellSky: Thoma Bravo → TPG (December 2016) → TPG + Leonard Green (July 2020, >$3B [EST]), now 5.5+ years into the current sponsor cycle. HHAeXchange: Cressey (2018) → Cressey + Hg (September 2021), 4.5 years in. Netsmart: Genstar → Allscripts/GI JV (2016) → GI + TA (2018), 7+ years in and attempting its second sale. **Every obvious strategic buyer in the space is itself within 12–24 months of pressure to monetize for its own LPs** — which both creates forcing-function buyer urgency and explains why bolt-on multiples have held up better than platform multiples.

**Typical founding-to-first-exit timeline is 10–15 years in home care SaaS**, with sponsor-to-sponsor cycles every 4–7 years thereafter. Careswitch (founded 2021, acquired 2025) at four years is a positive outlier, likely driven by AI narrative premium and Paradigm's strategic gap. ClearCare (founded 2010, sold 2019) at nine years is more typical. Founders should plan for **2–3 private transition stages** before a true liquidity event.

## Regulatory and capital catalysts create a narrow 2026–2027 window

Four regulatory forcing functions arrive in the next 18 months and each creates software replacement cycles that reward vendors with fresh compliance capability. **OASIS‑E2 implementation on April 2026** requires home health software updates, creating forced replacement cycles for legacy systems. The **80/20 Medicaid HCBS rule** (80% of HCBS payments must flow to direct care worker compensation) forces agencies to automate back-office, directly benefiting vertical SaaS that squeezes overhead. **EVV enforcement** has shifted to active audit mode — HHS-OIG audits flagged $14B in non-compliant New York spend, Mississippi implemented claim-suspension edits on January 1, 2026 — creating a massive forced-adoption catalyst for compliant EVV vendors. **Hospital-at-home waiver extensions** continue expanding the addressable care coordination market. The OBBBA Medicaid restructuring is the only meaningful headwind, with state-dependent coverage reductions possible under the new 1915(c) waiver type.

Capital conditions support deal-making despite narrow IPO windows. **Fed funds held at 3.50–3.75%** as of March 2026 with one cut signaled for 2026 and another for 2027; Powell's term expires May 15, 2026 and a new Chair could accelerate cuts. **Healthcare PE deal value hit a record $191B in 2025** (second-highest buyout count ever) with IT-specific deals roughly doubling year-over-year. **Digital health funding reached $14.2B across 482 deals in 2025** (up 35% from 2024), and **Q1 2026 at $4B** was the strongest Q1 since the pandemic peak. Sponsor-to-sponsor activity is returning and continuation vehicles are proliferating as alt-exits for PE holding cash-generative healthcare assets past fund life — a structural tailwind for home care SaaS sellers.

## Cautionary patterns every seller must understand

The **WellSky/ClearCare integration is the canonical cautionary tale** for founders evaluating strategic acquirers. Post-2019 acquisition, customer sentiment on KLAS, Capterra, TrustRadius, and Software Advice has consistently documented "highly dissatisfied with customer service," "no response to tickets," "AI features promised but never delivered," and most recently (December 2025+) **EVV non-compliance for over a month following the August 2025 Open EVV rollout in Tennessee**, with agencies reporting unpaid Medicaid claims described as "financial disaster." The pattern — post-acquisition R&D under-investment, support degradation, forced upsells — creates exactly the product-gap opportunity that enables new PD entrants and should be a diligence focus for any founder evaluating acquirer fit. It also directly informs multiple sellers' playbooks: **WellSky competitors are using ClearCare customer dissatisfaction as a primary sales motion**, and any acquirer will underwrite some portion of that defection risk into their valuation.

**Honor Technology's 2021–2024 trajectory** is the cautionary services/tech hybrid story: $1.25B Series E valuation (October 2021) on $70M equity + $300M debt, three rounds of layoffs (January 2020, June 2023, August 2023 WARN notices), franchisee relationship crisis, no subsequent priced equity round, and CEO admission that due diligence on Home Instead missed operational complexity. While no confirmed public down round exists, the absence of any post-2021 priced round amid a well-documented valuation reset across comparable assets is the functional equivalent. **Netsmart's 2024 failed sale** demonstrated that even $250M EBITDA, scaled HCIT platforms could not clear sponsor-to-sponsor bids at the target multiple in the 2024 environment.

Two further risk patterns deserve attention. First, **single-state Medicaid concentration** is an acute risk under OBBBA state-dependent coverage reductions — diversified multi-state exposure is worth a measurable multiple premium. Second, **single-product vs. platform positioning** is being repriced sharply: standalone scheduling or EVV point solutions are discounted heavily versus integrated platforms (agency management + EVV + workforce + billing + analytics).

## Conclusion: four actionable takeaways

The 2026 M&A window for private-duty home care SaaS is genuinely favorable but more disciplined than 2021. First, **target a PE sponsor-to-sponsor exit or strategic bolt-on at 5–10x ARR**, not an IPO and not a ServiceTitan-era premium; plan for 2–3 years of growth before exit and prepare a 20–35% earnout as part of consideration. Second, **build the Rule of 40 + AI-embedded-in-workflow story as a package**, not as separate narratives — the 10–20% healthcare discount to general cloud inverts to a premium only when both are present. Third, **focus buyer outreach on HHAeXchange, AlayaCare, WellSky, LivTech, Activated Insights, Axxess, and Paradigm** as the seven serial acquirers running active tuck-in programs in the exact target size range; Jonas/Constellation is a useful fallback for proprietary processes. Fourth, **treat regulatory timing as deal timing**: the convergence of OASIS-E2, 80/20 HCBS, EVV enforcement, and hospital-at-home expansion creates a coincident demand pull in 2026–2027 that is unlikely to recur; combined with aging sponsor portfolios at every obvious strategic buyer, this is the narrowest-but-clearest M&A window in the category since the 2020–2021 cycle.

The counterintuitive insight is that the **Netsmart 2024 failure and WellSky customer satisfaction decline are net-positive signals for well-positioned sellers** — they prove acquirers now must pay for quality (AI, customer NPS, compliance readiness, multi-state breadth) rather than scale alone, which rewards disciplined sub-scale vendors who have done the hard operational work that Honor and ClearCare did not. The sellers who will clear premium multiples in 2026 are those who can credibly differentiate against the WellSky narrative on support and product velocity, demonstrate EVV/OASIS-E2 readiness, and prove AI-driven margin expansion in a specific payer vertical (Medicaid HCBS, VA, private-pay, or Medicare Advantage home care benefits) — not those who chase the biggest TAM or the flashiest AI demo.
