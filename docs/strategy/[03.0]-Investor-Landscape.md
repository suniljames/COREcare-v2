# COREcare v2 Investor Landscape: Fundraising Playbook for Caregiver-Mobile-First SMB SaaS

**Canonical lane:** "Caregiver-Mobile-First Care Ops for Private-Duty SMBs"

COREcare v2's best fundraising path runs through vertical SaaS generalists, not health-tech specialists. The company targets a **$107B+ non-medical home care market** plagued by **79% caregiver turnover** and served by desktop-first incumbents that cannot cheaply retrofit mobile UX. With a $15K–$25K ARPA aimed at 5–200 ADC private-duty agencies, COREcare maps cleanly to the ServiceTitan/Toast/Gusto playbook — SMB vertical SaaS with mobile-first architecture, land-and-expand GTM, and embedded fintech optionality. Health-tech investors will pattern-match to the wrong archetype (enterprise clinical SaaS) and introduce friction around Medicare compliance, clinical validation, and payer strategy that simply don't apply here. The Q1 2026 landscape is unusually favorable: LivTech's Alora acquisition (April 9, 2026) opens a **6–12 month displacement window**, General Atlantic's $3B TEAM Services deal validates home care at premium multiples, and AI compression is slashing the team size needed to build category-defining vertical software.

---

## Why vertical SaaS investors beat health-tech specialists for this company

The structural argument is straightforward and evidence-backed. Health-tech investors build their entire diligence framework around three pillars: regulatory/clinical depth (FDA pathways, CMS reimbursement), enterprise health-system sales cycles (6–18 months), and clinical outcome metrics. COREcare has none of these. It targets **private-pay, non-medical agencies** — no Medicare billing, no clinical protocols, no FDA. When a health-tech IC evaluates COREcare, they'll ask about payer strategy, clinical advisory boards, and HIPAA beyond the basics. These are the wrong questions.

Vertical SaaS investors ask the right ones: **What's the caregiver mobile WAU?** What's CAC payback at $20K ARPA? Can you prove the Alora displacement motion? How does NRR expand as agencies add caregivers? The investment patterns confirm this. AxisCare took equity from **Frontier Growth**, a SaaS-focused growth firm — not a health-tech fund. ServiceTitan's journey from SMB home-services platform to $7.6B IPO was backed by Bessemer and Iconiq, not health-tech specialists. Toast's restaurant-ops-to-fintech evolution was funded by Bessemer, Tiger Global, and Addition — generalists who understood SMB GTM.

There's also a practical risk: health-tech investors may push COREcare toward Medicare-certified home health or clinical documentation, diluting the product focus and adding regulatory burden. Vertical SaaS investors reinforce the wedge: nail the mobile caregiver experience, expand the product surface, add payments/payroll, and grow via network effects. **The exit comps validate this framing** — vertical SaaS platforms trade at 5–9x NTM revenue versus 3–4x for generic health-tech, and vertical AI companies command **5.4x multiples** with a clear premium.

## Investment criteria: what investors will evaluate

**Market timing (the "why now" case):**

The confluence is unusually strong. **79% caregiver turnover** (2023 Activated Insights) means agencies are desperate for tools that reduce the 70% of new hires who quit within 100 days. PE roll-up activity is creating displacement windows — LivTech/Alora (April 2026), Paradigm/Careswitch (September 2025), and AxisCare/Frontier (January 2026) all force agencies to re-evaluate software. CMS extended the hospital-at-home waiver through **September 30, 2030**, with **366+ programs across 139 health systems** creating downstream demand for in-home care coordination. OASIS-E2 went live April 1, 2026, creating another switching moment as agencies evaluate EHR vendor readiness.

**Unit economics benchmarks COREcare must hit:**

| Metric | Target | Benchmark context |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| ARPA | $15K–$25K/yr | Healthy SMB zone; high enough for sustainable economics, low enough for volume |
| Gross margin | 75–85% (software-only) | Public SaaS median ~78%; AI inference costs may push to 65–75% |
| CAC | $500–$2K (small), $3–10K (mid) | SMB SaaS with <$5K ACV: median 9-month payback |
| CAC payback | <12 months | Overall SaaS median is 18–20 months, but SMB segment achieves 9 months |
| LTV:CAC | 3:1+ | Median 3.6:1 (Benchmarkit 2024); at $20K ARPA and 80% GM: LTV ≈ $107K |
| Logo retention | 85%+ annual | SMB churn runs 8.2x higher than enterprise; 85%+ is ambitious but required |
| NRR | 105–120% | Private B2B SaaS median 101–106%; 105%+ requires active expansion motion |

**Team composition investors expect:** Domain expertise in home care operations (ideally an operator who's run a 50+ ADC agency), mobile-native engineering leadership (React Native or Flutter, not Xamarin), SMB go-to-market experience (product-led or low-touch sales), and HIPAA/compliance depth embedded in architecture (RLS-enforced multi-tenancy, audit-first design). AI Series A teams now average **16.8 employees** (down from 25.9 in 2021) — lean is the new normal.

## Practical fundraising strategy

**Seed round (now):** Target **$2.5–4M on $12–18M post-money** via post-money SAFEs. Lead with a vertical SaaS specialist — Homebrew, Primary, or Bessemer are the top three choices in priority order. Homebrew's "Bottom Up Economy" thesis and Gusto portfolio make the pattern-match immediate; Primary's $625M Fund V and operational support team are ideal for a seed-stage company; Bessemer's "non-desk workers" thesis is textbook but harder to access. Fill the round with **ADP Ventures** or **Intuit Ventures** as a strategic co-investor, plus 2–3 angels: home care agency operators, former WellSky/AxisCare product executives, and vertical SaaS founders who've run the SMB playbook.

**Series A (18–24 months out):** Target **$8–15M on $40–70M post-money** with proof points: **$2–5M ARR, 100–300 logos, logo churn <10%, caregiver mobile WAU ≥85% of scheduled caregivers, CAC payback <12 months, NRR 105–115%, and at least 15–20 Alora displacement wins**. Lead candidates: Battery Ventures (Chelsea Stoner's healthcare IT + vertical SaaS combo), Scale Venture Partners (explicitly targets $500K–$5M ARR companies in low-tech industries), or Bessemer (if they didn't lead seed). Emergence Capital's $1B Fund VII and "deskless workforce" thesis make them an excellent alternative lead.

**Growth narrative (36+ months):** Expand into adjacent verticals — HCBS/IDD waiver services, pediatric home care, and light hospice intake workflows. Deepen geographic penetration in Texas, Florida, and California (largest agency markets). Build the embedded fintech layer (payroll integration via ADP, invoicing via QuickBooks, caregiver earned-wage access). At $15M+ ARR, General Atlantic becomes the most strategically significant growth investor — their $3B TEAM Services acquisition means they need digitized agency infrastructure across 100,000 caregivers. Vista's Endeavor fund ($850M, targeting <$30M ARR software) is the operational-excellence growth path.

## Key takeaways

**The SMB vertical SaaS lens wins.** COREcare's non-medical, non-Medicare, caregiver-UX-led positioning maps to Homebrew's "Bottom Up Economy," Bessemer's "non-desk workers," and Emergence's "deskless workforce" — not to Rock Health's clinical digital health or Define Ventures' health-system buyers. Every major exit comp in this pattern (ServiceTitan at $7.6B, Toast at $15B+, Procore at $9B) was funded by generalist vertical SaaS investors. Health-tech funds will undervalue the opportunity because their evaluation rubric overweights regulatory moats and clinical depth that don't exist in this lane.

**The competitive landscape is crowded but structurally vulnerable.** WellSky, AxisCare, and AlayaCare dominate but are desktop-charting-first architectures backed by PE firms optimizing for financial engineering, not product innovation. The April 2026 LivTech/Alora acquisition creates a concrete displacement window — agencies facing migration uncertainty will evaluate alternatives for the next 6–12 months. AxisCare's Frontier equity and Intelligence AI launch signal they recognize the mobile/AI threat but are retrofitting, not rebuilding.

**Three metrics will make or break the fundraise.** First, **caregiver mobile WAU ≥85%** of scheduled caregivers — this is the single most differentiated metric COREcare can show and no competitor tracks it publicly. Second, **Alora displacement wins** — converting 15–20 agencies from LivTech/Alora's disrupted base proves the thesis in concrete terms. Third, **CAC payback <12 months** — SMB vertical SaaS with $15–25K ARPA should achieve 9-month payback through product-led or low-touch acquisition, and this number signals capital efficiency to investors who've been burned by high-CAC health-tech.

**Optimal sequencing:** Seed led by Homebrew or Primary Venture Partners with an ADP Ventures or Intuit Ventures strategic check, targeting $2.5–4M. Use the 18-month wedge to hit $1.5–3M ARR with 50–100 logos. Series A led by Battery Ventures or Scale Venture Partners at $8–15M, with Bessemer or Emergence as alternative leads. Growth via General Atlantic relationship (TEAM Services creates a natural strategic conversation) or Vista Endeavor fund. The entire capital strategy should be communicated as a vertical SaaS story with healthcare tailwinds — not a health-tech story with SaaS characteristics.
